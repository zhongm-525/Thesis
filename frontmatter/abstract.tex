\chapter{Abstract}
\thispagestyle{plain}

\noindent This thesis studies how AI-based methods compute actuation commands that drive a pneumatic soft manipulator's tip to a desired position---the inverse-kinematics problem, which is the standard enabling technology for open-loop position control of a continuum arm. Four learning-based families (feed-forward, recurrent, Jacobian-learned iteration, and sim-to-real fine-tuning) are compared head-to-head against classical piecewise-constant-curvature inversion, on two pneumatic datasets with complementary actuation dimensionality: a six-chamber two-module I-Support recording and an undocumented three-valve recording. A paired mid-marker ablation quantifies partial-shape sensing on the over-actuated platform; a PyElastica sim-to-real study evaluates pre-training at four real-data budgets; a four-trajectory tracking protocol applies an identical evaluation to every method through a shared learned forward-kinematics arbiter. No closed-loop experiments on physical hardware are reported.

Classical piecewise-constant-curvature inverse kinematics is not competitive with a small feed-forward network on either dataset, failing for distinct reasons: a constant-arc-length structural ceiling produces a $22$\,mm residual on the three-valve recording, and a multi-valued-inverse calibration collapse produces a $51$\,mm closed-loop error on the two-module recording despite a near-zero geometric residual. Adding a mid-marker triad to the two-module input raises the pressure-space coefficient of determination from $0.32$ to $0.51$, the largest single-factor improvement observed. The warm-started Jacobian-learned iteration reaches $0.77$\,mm mean closed-loop tracking error on the two-module recording, matching the sub-millimetre order of magnitude reported for learned-Jacobian inverse kinematics on other pneumatic platforms. Sim-to-real pre-training gives no measurable benefit at any real-data budget on the three-valve recording, a negative result attributable to a fivefold synthetic--real workspace mismatch rather than to sample count.

No single method dominates every evaluation regime. Inverse-kinematics method choice for pneumatic soft robots depends on three physical properties of the platform and task---actuation redundancy, sensing coverage, and the overlap between any simulator prior and the real operating workspace---and this three-property criterion organises the regime-specific findings into a compact method-selection framework.
