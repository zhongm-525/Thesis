\chapter{Abstract}
\thispagestyle{plain}

\noindent\textbf{Background.} Inverse kinematics---mapping a target tip position to chamber actuations---is the enabling technology for open-loop position control of pneumatic soft manipulators. Classical piecewise-constant-curvature (PCC) inversion is undermined by large strains and multi-valued inverses, and learning-based inverse-kinematics families are rarely compared on the same platform under a common protocol.

\textbf{Methods.} Four learning families (feed-forward, recurrent, Jacobian-learned iteration, and sim-to-real fine-tuning) were benchmarked against PCC inversion on two pneumatic datasets with complementary actuation dimensionality: a six-chamber two-module I-Support recording and an undocumented three-valve recording. A paired mid-marker ablation quantified partial-shape sensing on the over-actuated platform; a PyElastica sim-to-real study evaluated pre-training at four real-data budgets; a shared learned forward-kinematics arbiter applied an identical four-trajectory tracking protocol to every method. No closed-loop experiments on physical hardware were performed.

\textbf{Results.} PCC was uncompetitive with a small feed-forward network on either dataset: a constant-arc-length ceiling produced a $22$\,mm residual on the three-valve recording, and a multi-valued-inverse calibration collapse produced a $51$\,mm closed-loop error on the two-module recording. Adding a mid-marker triad raised the two-module coefficient of determination from $0.32$ to $0.51$. The warm-started Jacobian-learned iteration reached $0.77$\,mm mean closed-loop error on the two-module recording. Sim-to-real pre-training gave no measurable benefit at any real-data budget, attributable to a fivefold workspace mismatch.

\textbf{Conclusions.} No single method dominated. Method choice depended on three platform properties---actuation redundancy, sensing coverage, and simulator--real workspace overlap---which together formed a compact method-selection framework.
