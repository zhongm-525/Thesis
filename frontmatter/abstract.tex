\chapter{Abstract}
\thispagestyle{plain}

\noindent This thesis, titled \emph{AI for Control of Soft Robot by Using ML and Inverse Kinematics}, studies how AI-based methods compute actuation commands that drive a pneumatic soft manipulator's tip to a desired position---the inverse-kinematics (IK) problem, which is the standard enabling technology for open-loop position control of a continuum arm in the soft-robotics literature \cite{giorelli2015nn, ma2022bp, fang2022jacobian, centurelli2022closed}. In this work, \emph{AI} and \emph{ML} are instantiated as four learning-based families (feed-forward, recurrent, Jacobian-learned iteration, and sim-to-real fine-tuning), \emph{Inverse Kinematics} is instantiated as the classical analytical piecewise-constant-curvature baseline together with the hybrid Jacobian-learned iteration (which combines an ML forward model with an iterative inverse solver), and \emph{Control} is interpreted as the open-loop form that maps a target tip pose to a pressure command and is validated by four-trajectory tracking through a shared learned forward-kinematics arbiter. No closed-loop experiments on physical hardware are reported; the scope is the offline study of the AI-for-IK enabling step that any closed-loop controller would invoke.

\noindent\textbf{Background:}
Pneumatic soft manipulators trade the precision of rigid-link arms for material compliance, which makes them attractive for safe human interaction but hard to control. The inverse-kinematics problem---computing the actuation commands that drive the tip to a target position---is complicated by a non-linear pressure-to-deformation map, viscoelastic hysteresis, and, on multi-segment arms, a multi-valued inverse that no single-branch model can capture. Four method families---analytical piecewise-constant-curvature inversion, feed-forward neural regression, recurrent neural regression, and Jacobian-learned iteration---have been proposed independently, but each has been evaluated on the platform its authors introduced, and no prior study has compared them side by side on identical data.

\noindent\textbf{Methods:}
This thesis reports a head-to-head empirical comparison of the four families on two pneumatic datasets with complementary actuation dimensionality: a six-chamber two-module I-Support recording (Dataset 1) and an undocumented three-valve recording (Dataset 2). A paired mid-marker ablation quantifies the value of partial-shape sensing on the over-actuated platform; a PyElastica-based sim-to-real study evaluates pre-training transfer at four real-data budgets; and a four-trajectory tracking protocol applies an identical evaluation to every method through a shared learned forward-kinematics arbiter.

\noindent\textbf{Results:}
The analytical baseline is not competitive with even a small feed-forward network on either dataset, failing for distinct reasons: a constant-arc-length structural ceiling produces a $22$\,mm residual on Dataset 2, and a multi-valued-inverse calibration collapse produces a $51$\,mm closed-loop error on Dataset 1. Adding mid-marker input to the Dataset 1 network raises the pressure-space coefficient of determination from $0.32$ to $0.51$, the largest single-factor improvement observed. The warm-started Jacobian-learned iteration reaches $0.77$\,mm mean closed-loop tracking error on the Dataset 1 reference trajectories, matching the sub-millimetre order of magnitude reported by Fang et al.\ (2022). Sim-to-real pre-training produces no measurable improvement on Dataset 2 at any real-data budget, a negative result attributable to a fivefold synthetic--real workspace mismatch rather than to sample-count limits.

\noindent\textbf{Conclusions:}
No single method dominates every evaluation regime. The choice of inverse-kinematics method for a new pneumatic soft-robot arm depends on three physical properties of the platform and task---actuation redundancy, sensing coverage, and the overlap between any simulator prior and the real operating workspace---and this three-property criterion organises the regime-specific findings into a compact method-selection framework for practitioners.
