\section{Data Exploration Findings}
\label{sec:eda_findings}

The exploratory analysis of Section~\ref{sec:eda} produced findings on each dataset that guided the modelling and preprocessing decisions used in the remainder of the thesis. Dataset 1 findings are reported in Section~\ref{sec:eda_ds1} and Dataset 2 findings in Section~\ref{sec:eda_ds2}.

\subsection{Dataset 1}
\label{sec:eda_ds1}

\paragraph{Data integrity and occlusion.}
All six recordings of Dataset 1 contained exactly $4{,}000$ raw frames, giving $24{,}000$ frames before preprocessing. Marker occlusion was negligible (Table~\ref{tab:eda_ds1_occlusion}): the six recordings lost between $0$ and $34$ frames each to marker drop-outs, for a total of $64$ lost frames ($0.27\%$ of the raw total). The mid-marker and tip-marker occlusion masks were identical in every recording, so the same $23{,}936$ frames remain usable whether the tip-only or the tip-plus-mid input is selected.

\paragraph{Base stationarity.}
The per-frame standard deviation of the base-marker mean position was computed for each recording. Five of the six recordings showed apparent base motion of up to $\sim$$32$\,mm, but inspection of the raw marker stream confirmed that this was an artefact of the NaN-aware mean: when one or two of the three base markers were momentarily occluded, the mean collapsed onto the remaining marker's offset from the cluster centroid and inflated the apparent deviation. Once frames with any occluded marker were removed, the standard deviation fell below the optical marker resolution. The base was therefore treated as fixed within every recording, and this finding supports the translation-only preprocessing used in Section~\ref{sec:splits}.

\paragraph{Cross-seed workspace overlap.}
The base-relative tip point clouds of the four seeds overlap within a $\sim$$12$\,mm radius of each other (Fig~\ref{fig:ds1_workspace}), which is small compared with the $\sim$$48$\,mm intra-seed tip-position spread. This confirmation supports the decision to use a simple per-frame base subtraction rather than the fuller local-frame rotation that would have been needed had the seeds shown rotational drift between recording sessions.

\paragraph{Tip and mid magnitudes.}
The mean base-relative tip magnitude was approximately $375$\,mm, with a maximum of $\sim$$405$\,mm, consistent with the $\sim$$410$\,mm assembled length of the I-Support arm. The mean mid-marker magnitude was approximately $198$\,mm, consistent with the mid-section sitting near the midpoint of the two-segment arm. Both quantities fall well within the $500$\,mm sanity threshold used in the loader's unit tests, so no frame was rejected on magnitude grounds.

\paragraph{Seed-0 repeatability.}
The three repetitions of the seed-0 random-walk were used to estimate the physical noise floor of the tip measurement. For each tip sample in one repetition, the nearest neighbour in each of the other two repetitions was located in the six-dimensional pressure-command space, and the standard deviation of the three paired tip positions was recorded per frame. The resulting distribution (Fig~\ref{fig:ds1_repeatability}) had a mean of $2.29$\,mm, a median of $1.88$\,mm, and a ninety-fifth percentile of $4.14$\,mm, with a long tail out to $60.3$\,mm driven by a handful of outliers coincident with brief occlusion gaps. The $\sim$$2.3$\,mm mean is taken as the physical noise floor of the Dataset 1 closed-loop error in the rest of this chapter: an inverse-kinematics method achieving a lower closed-loop error on Dataset 1 would be fitting measurement noise rather than the underlying actuation--pose relationship.

\paragraph{Pressure-command variation across seed-0 repetitions.}
Contrary to the expectation set by a shared random-number-generator seed, the pressure-command sequences of the three seed-0 repetitions were not bit-identical. The mean per-frame Euclidean distance between the first and second repetitions was $13.2$ raw units, and between the first and third repetitions was $45.2$ raw units, on a command range of $0$--$100$ raw units. Pairing frames by recording index would therefore compare slightly different pressure commands; the nearest-neighbour matching used above is the robust alternative and was adopted for the repeatability statistic.

\subsection{Dataset 2}
\label{sec:eda_ds2}

\paragraph{Data integrity.}
Dataset 2 contains $18{,}072$ rows, with no missing values and no duplicate rows. The three valve commands are integer-valued over the interval $[0, 2500]$, and no row lies outside this band. After the inch-to-millimetre conversion of Section~\ref{sec:ds2}, the tip coordinates spanned $[-47.9, 53.8]$\,mm in $X$, $[-47.7, 56.1]$\,mm in $Y$, and $[-27.5, 36.9]$\,mm in $Z$, giving the flat disc-shaped workspace of approximately $100$\,mm width and $65$\,mm depth shown in Fig~\ref{fig:ds2_workspace}.

\paragraph{Absence of temporal structure.}
The step-size statistic described in Section~\ref{sec:eda} was used to test whether consecutive rows carry any temporal dependency. The mean Euclidean step between consecutive rows in tip-position space was $32.49$\,mm, and the mean step after shuffling the rows was $33.84$\,mm, giving a ratio of $0.96$. The analogous ratio in the three-dimensional valve-command space was $1.003$. Both ratios are indistinguishable from unity. The rows of Dataset 2 therefore behave as statistically independent samples drawn from a fixed distribution rather than as points along a trajectory. Dataset 2 was treated as an IID collection in the remainder of the thesis. The $70/15/15$ recording-order split of Section~\ref{sec:splits} was kept as the default, both to preserve the option of detecting any subtle residual temporal structure in a downstream experiment and to comply with the original data-handling protocol.

\paragraph{Actuation--pose correlation.}
Pearson correlations between each valve command and each tip coordinate are shown in Fig~\ref{fig:ds2_correlation}. Every valve--coordinate pair has a Pearson coefficient with magnitude above $0.09$, and the strongest correlations are consistent with a three-chamber radial arrangement at $120^{\circ}$ spacing: valve~1 pulls the tip along $-Y$ and $+Z$, valve~2 along $+X$, and valve~3 along $-X$. The mapping from actuation to pose is therefore clearly non-degenerate, which is the minimal precondition for an inverse-kinematics model to find structure in the dataset.

\section{PCC Analytical Baseline (Exp 1)}

\subsection{Single-segment PCC on Dataset 2}
\label{sec:res_pcc_single}

The single-segment PCC baseline of Section~\ref{sec:pcc_single} was calibrated on the $12{,}650$-frame training split of Dataset 2 and evaluated on the $2{,}712$-frame test split. This subsection reports the calibration outcome, the test-set metrics, and the diagnostic analysis that attributes the remaining error to structural limitations of the single-segment constant-length PCC model rather than to the linear length-to-actuation fit.

\paragraph{Calibration.}
The reference-length scan described in Section~\ref{sec:pcc_single} selected $L_0 = 67.24$\,mm, corresponding to $1.05 \times \max_k \| \mathbf{p}_k \|$ on the training tip positions. The linear length-to-actuation fit gave per-chamber slopes $\mathbf{a} \approx (31.7,\ 23.2,\ 18.9)$ in valve-command units per millimetre of chamber-length change, and intercepts $\mathbf{b} \approx (1927,\ 1703,\ 1941)$ in valve-command units. The three slopes differ by up to a factor of $1.7$, consistent with chamber-specific gains in the physical actuation system that the per-chamber linear fit absorbs independently.

\paragraph{Headline metrics.}
Table~\ref{tab:res_pcc_single} summarises the test-set performance. On the $1{,}385$ test targets that lie inside the single-segment PCC workspace ($51\%$ of the full test set), the predicted actuation produces a mean tip residual of $22.67$\,mm when passed back through the forward map---an order of magnitude above the physical marker noise observed on Dataset 1 (Section~\ref{sec:eda_ds1}) and more than twice the $10$\,mm scale one would associate with the $5\%$ MAPE target reported by Ma et al.\ \cite{ma2022bp} on a similar three-chamber arm. Over the full test set, $48.9\%$ of targets fail the workspace condition and receive the penalised value equal to the workspace diameter (${\sim}155$\,mm), giving a penalised mean of $87.64$\,mm. The distribution of per-target forward residuals on the reachable subset is shown in Fig~\ref{fig:res_pcc_error}: the distribution is unimodal but broad, extending well past the mean into a long tail.

\paragraph{Frame-mismatch diagnostic.}
A first hypothesis for the large residual and the low reachability was that Dataset 2's recorded origin lies near the robot's straight-pose tip rather than at its base, which would place reachable PCC targets in a different coordinate frame from the one assumed by Eqs.~\eqref{eq:pcc_phi}--\eqref{eq:pcc_L}. The nominally-straight rows of the training split, identified as those for which the cross-chamber standard deviation of the valve command was below $50$, had a tip centroid of $(10.6, -5.8, -4.1)$\,mm rather than the expected $(0, 0, L_0)$, which confirmed the frame offset. Three frame-transform variants were then evaluated on the validation split (Table~\ref{tab:res_pcc_frame}): the raw frame, a pure translation that maps the straight-pose tip to $(0, 0, L_0)$, and a translation combined with a $z$-axis flip. The flip-and-translation variant raised the reachable fraction from $51\%$ to $99.6\%$, but it reduced the mean forward residual by only $1.7$\,mm. Frame misalignment therefore accounts for the reachability artefact, but not for the residual.

\paragraph{Elongation diagnostic.}
A second hypothesis was that the arm elongates under pressurisation, which a single-segment constant-length PCC has no degree of freedom to represent. Fig~\ref{fig:res_pcc_elongation} plots the training-split tip norm against two actuation summaries: the sum of the three valve commands, as a proxy for total pressurisation, and the cross-chamber standard deviation of the commands, as a proxy for bending magnitude. The tip norm correlates with total pressurisation at Pearson $r = +0.737$, and with the bending proxy at only $r = +0.429$. The elongation signal therefore dominates the bending signal in this workspace, and the constant arc length $L$ assumed in Eq.~\eqref{eq:pcc_L} is unable to absorb it. This is the structural ceiling on the reachable-subset forward residual.

Right: tip norm against the cross-chamber standard deviation of the valve commands, a proxy for bending magnitude ($r = +0.429$). \paragraph{Interpretation.}
Taken together, the frame and elongation diagnostics indicate that the single-segment constant-length PCC can reach almost every target in the Dataset 2 workspace once the frame is corrected, but it cannot reproduce the pressure-induced elongation implied by the $r = +0.737$ correlation. The remaining ${\sim}22$\,mm mean residual on the reachable subset is therefore not a deficiency of the linear length-to-actuation fit but a ceiling imposed by the model class itself. This finding motivates the data-driven inverse-kinematics methods reported in the following sections, which do not commit to a fixed arc length and can therefore absorb the elongation coupling implicitly from the training data.

\subsection{Two-segment PCC on Dataset 1}
\label{sec:res_pcc_multi}

The two-segment PCC pipeline of Section~\ref{sec:pcc_multi} was calibrated on the $7{,}981$-frame training split of Dataset 1 and evaluated on the $11{,}956$-frame test split through the closed-loop protocol: each test target was passed through the SLSQP inverse, the linear length-to-pressure fit, and the forward kinematics of Eq.~\eqref{eq:pcc_multi_fk}, and the Euclidean distance to the target was recorded under the learned arbiter of Section~\ref{sec:jacobian}. The outcome is a clean negative result: the geometric inverse fits almost exactly, but the linear length-to-pressure calibration collapses and drives the closed-loop error above what even a small feed-forward network achieves on the same targets.

\paragraph{Geometric inverse is essentially exact.}
The SLSQP residual $\| \mathbf{x}^{*} - \mathbf{p}_{\mathrm{tip}}(\boldsymbol{\xi}) \|$ at convergence---measured \emph{before} the pressure calibration of Eq.~\eqref{eq:pcc_multi_linear} is applied---averaged $3.5 \times 10^{-3}$\,mm across the test set, with a $95$th percentile of $2.1 \times 10^{-4}$\,mm and a long-tail maximum of $10.2$\,mm attributable to the small fraction of targets at which SLSQP returned without fully converging. Every Dataset 1 tip target therefore admits a two-segment PCC configuration $\boldsymbol{\xi}$ that reproduces it to below the marker-noise floor. The warm-started SLSQP converged in a median of three iterations per target, and the per-target wall-clock time averaged $3$\,ms on the CPU.

\paragraph{Closed-loop error.}
Once the SLSQP configuration is mapped to chamber pressures by the linear fit and those pressures are passed through the arbiter forward map, the closed-loop position error rises to a mean of $51.22$\,mm with a $95$th percentile of $91.55$\,mm and a maximum of $165.63$\,mm (Table~\ref{tab:res_pcc_multi_vs_mlp}). The tip-only feed-forward network of Section~\ref{sec:mlp_tip}, evaluated on the same targets through the same arbiter, reaches a mean closed-loop error of $13.04$\,mm---a factor of $3.9$ smaller than PCC. Pressure-space metrics tell the same story: the two-segment PCC pipeline's coefficient of determination against the ground-truth chamber pressures is slightly negative ($R^{2} = -0.02$), so the linear length-to-pressure calibration explains no variance beyond predicting the per-chamber mean, whereas the feed-forward network achieves $R^{2} = +0.32$. Fig~\ref{fig:res_pcc_multi_compare} illustrates the contrast: the two distributions of closed-loop error are well separated on a logarithmic axis, and the per-chamber pressure residuals of the PCC pipeline sit consistently above those of the feed-forward network.

\paragraph{Failure mechanism.}
The combination of a near-zero geometric residual with a $51$\,mm closed-loop error is explained by the interaction between the multi-valued inverse and the linear length-to-pressure map. Dataset 1's six-chamber actuation is mapped into a three-dimensional tip position, so the inverse of Eq.~\eqref{eq:pcc_multi_fk} admits a continuous family of configurations for each target. The SLSQP solver picks out one member of this family per target, and the pressure calibration of Eq.~\eqref{eq:pcc_multi_linear} is fit by ordinary least squares on the training frames' $(\ell_{i}, V_{i})$ pairs. Because SLSQP can land on different solution branches for training frames whose tips are close together, the per-chamber scatter of $V_{i}$ at any given chamber length is large, and the least-squares fit regresses almost to the per-chamber mean ($R^{2} \approx 0$ per chamber). The calibrated pressures that the pipeline returns for a test target are therefore close to the training-set mean pressure rather than to the specific pressure that would have produced the target.

\paragraph{Interpretation.}
Together with the $22$\,mm structural ceiling on Dataset 2 (Section~\ref{sec:res_pcc_single}), this result shows that classical piecewise-constant-curvature inverse kinematics is not competitive with even a small feed-forward network on either of the two datasets used in this thesis. On Dataset 2, the constant-arc-length assumption is the bottleneck. On Dataset 1, that assumption holds well enough---the geometric inverse is essentially exact---but the multi-valued inverse combined with a linear pressure calibration destroys the connection between recovered chamber lengths and the actuation command that produced them. Two-segment PCC therefore serves in the remainder of this thesis as a classical baseline whose failure mode motivates the data-driven approaches of the following sections, rather than as a competitive method in its own right.
