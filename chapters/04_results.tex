\section{Data Exploration Findings}
\label{sec:eda_findings}

The exploratory analysis of Section~\ref{sec:eda} produced findings on each dataset that guided the modelling and preprocessing decisions used in the remainder of the thesis. Dataset 1 findings are reported in Section~\ref{sec:eda_ds1} and Dataset 2 findings in Section~\ref{sec:eda_ds2}.

\subsection{Dataset 1}
\label{sec:eda_ds1}

\paragraph{Data integrity and occlusion.}
All six recordings of Dataset 1 contained exactly $4{,}000$ raw frames, giving $24{,}000$ frames before preprocessing. Marker occlusion was negligible (Table~\ref{tab:eda_ds1_occlusion}): the six recordings lost between $0$ and $34$ frames each to marker drop-outs, for a total of $64$ lost frames ($0.27\%$ of the raw total). The mid-marker and tip-marker occlusion masks were identical in every recording, so the same $23{,}936$ frames remain usable whether the tip-only or the tip-plus-mid input is selected.

\paragraph{Base stationarity.}
The per-frame standard deviation of the base-marker mean position was computed for each recording. Five of the six recordings showed apparent base motion of up to $\sim$$32$\,mm, but inspection of the raw marker stream confirmed that this was an artefact of the NaN-aware mean: when one or two of the three base markers were momentarily occluded, the mean collapsed onto the remaining marker's offset from the cluster centroid and inflated the apparent deviation. Once frames with any occluded marker were removed, the standard deviation fell below the optical marker resolution. The base was therefore treated as fixed within every recording, and this finding supports the translation-only preprocessing used in Section~\ref{sec:splits}.

\paragraph{Cross-seed workspace overlap.}
The base-relative tip point clouds of the four seeds overlap within a $\sim$$12$\,mm radius of each other (Fig~\ref{fig:ds1_workspace}), which is small compared with the $\sim$$48$\,mm intra-seed tip-position spread. This confirmation supports the decision to use a simple per-frame base subtraction rather than the fuller local-frame rotation that would have been needed had the seeds shown rotational drift between recording sessions.

\paragraph{Tip and mid magnitudes.}
The mean base-relative tip magnitude was approximately $375$\,mm, with a maximum of $\sim$$405$\,mm, consistent with the $\sim$$410$\,mm assembled length of the I-Support arm. The mean mid-marker magnitude was approximately $198$\,mm, consistent with the mid-section sitting near the midpoint of the two-segment arm. Both quantities fall well within the $500$\,mm sanity threshold used in the loader's unit tests, so no frame was rejected on magnitude grounds.

\paragraph{Seed-0 repeatability.}
The three repetitions of the seed-0 random-walk were used to estimate the physical noise floor of the tip measurement. For each tip sample in one repetition, the nearest neighbour in each of the other two repetitions was located in the six-dimensional pressure-command space, and the standard deviation of the three paired tip positions was recorded per frame. The resulting distribution (Fig~\ref{fig:ds1_repeatability}) had a mean of $2.29$\,mm, a median of $1.88$\,mm, and a ninety-fifth percentile of $4.14$\,mm, with a long tail out to $60.3$\,mm driven by a handful of outliers coincident with brief occlusion gaps. The $\sim$$2.3$\,mm mean is taken as the physical noise floor of the Dataset 1 closed-loop error in the rest of this chapter: an inverse-kinematics method achieving a lower closed-loop error on Dataset 1 would be fitting measurement noise rather than the underlying actuation--pose relationship.

\paragraph{Pressure-command variation across seed-0 repetitions.}
Contrary to the expectation set by a shared random-number-generator seed, the pressure-command sequences of the three seed-0 repetitions were not bit-identical. The mean per-frame Euclidean distance between the first and second repetitions was $13.2$ raw units, and between the first and third repetitions was $45.2$ raw units, on a command range of $0$--$100$ raw units. Pairing frames by recording index would therefore compare slightly different pressure commands; the nearest-neighbour matching used above is the robust alternative and was adopted for the repeatability statistic.

\subsection{Dataset 2}
\label{sec:eda_ds2}

\paragraph{Data integrity.}
Dataset 2 contains $18{,}072$ rows, with no missing values and no duplicate rows. The three valve commands are integer-valued over the interval $[0, 2500]$, and no row lies outside this band. After the inch-to-millimetre conversion of Section~\ref{sec:ds2}, the tip coordinates spanned $[-47.9, 53.8]$\,mm in $X$, $[-47.7, 56.1]$\,mm in $Y$, and $[-27.5, 36.9]$\,mm in $Z$, giving the flat disc-shaped workspace of approximately $100$\,mm width and $65$\,mm depth shown in Fig~\ref{fig:ds2_workspace}.

\paragraph{Absence of temporal structure.}
The step-size statistic described in Section~\ref{sec:eda} was used to test whether consecutive rows carry any temporal dependency. The mean Euclidean step between consecutive rows in tip-position space was $32.49$\,mm, and the mean step after shuffling the rows was $33.84$\,mm, giving a ratio of $0.96$. The analogous ratio in the three-dimensional valve-command space was $1.003$. Both ratios are indistinguishable from unity. The rows of Dataset 2 therefore behave as statistically independent samples drawn from a fixed distribution rather than as points along a trajectory. Dataset 2 was treated as an IID collection in the remainder of the thesis. The $70/15/15$ recording-order split of Section~\ref{sec:splits} was kept as the default, both to preserve the option of detecting any subtle residual temporal structure in a downstream experiment and to comply with the original data-handling protocol.

\paragraph{Actuation--pose correlation.}
Pearson correlations between each valve command and each tip coordinate are shown in Fig~\ref{fig:ds2_correlation}. Every valve--coordinate pair has a Pearson coefficient with magnitude above $0.09$, and the strongest correlations are consistent with a three-chamber radial arrangement at $120^{\circ}$ spacing: valve~1 pulls the tip along $-Y$ and $+Z$, valve~2 along $+X$, and valve~3 along $-X$. The mapping from actuation to pose is therefore clearly non-degenerate, which is the minimal precondition for an inverse-kinematics model to find structure in the dataset.

\section{PCC Analytical Baseline (Exp 1)}

\subsection{Single-segment PCC on Dataset 2}
\label{sec:res_pcc_single}

The single-segment PCC baseline of Section~\ref{sec:pcc_single} was calibrated on the $12{,}650$-frame training split of Dataset 2 and evaluated on the $2{,}712$-frame test split. This subsection reports the calibration outcome, the test-set metrics, and the diagnostic analysis that attributes the remaining error to structural limitations of the single-segment constant-length PCC model rather than to the linear length-to-actuation fit.

\paragraph{Calibration.}
The reference-length scan described in Section~\ref{sec:pcc_single} selected $L_0 = 67.24$\,mm, corresponding to $1.05 \times \max_k \| \mathbf{p}_k \|$ on the training tip positions. The linear length-to-actuation fit gave per-chamber slopes $\mathbf{a} \approx (31.7,\ 23.2,\ 18.9)$ in valve-command units per millimetre of chamber-length change, and intercepts $\mathbf{b} \approx (1927,\ 1703,\ 1941)$ in valve-command units. The three slopes differ by up to a factor of $1.7$, consistent with chamber-specific gains in the physical actuation system that the per-chamber linear fit absorbs independently.

\paragraph{Headline metrics.}
Table~\ref{tab:res_pcc_single} summarises the test-set performance. On the $1{,}385$ test targets that lie inside the single-segment PCC workspace ($51\%$ of the full test set), the predicted actuation produces a mean tip residual of $22.67$\,mm when passed back through the forward map---an order of magnitude above the physical marker noise observed on Dataset 1 (Section~\ref{sec:eda_ds1}) and more than twice the $10$\,mm scale one would associate with the $5\%$ MAPE target reported by Ma et al.\ \cite{ma2022bp} on a similar three-chamber arm. Over the full test set, $48.9\%$ of targets fail the workspace condition and receive the penalised value equal to the workspace diameter (${\sim}155$\,mm), giving a penalised mean of $87.64$\,mm. The distribution of per-target forward residuals on the reachable subset is shown in Fig~\ref{fig:res_pcc_error}: the distribution is unimodal but broad, extending well past the mean into a long tail.

\paragraph{Frame-mismatch diagnostic.}
A first hypothesis for the large residual and the low reachability was that Dataset 2's recorded origin lies near the robot's straight-pose tip rather than at its base, which would place reachable PCC targets in a different coordinate frame from the one assumed by Eqs.~\eqref{eq:pcc_phi}--\eqref{eq:pcc_L}. The nominally-straight rows of the training split, identified as those for which the cross-chamber standard deviation of the valve command was below $50$, had a tip centroid of $(10.6, -5.8, -4.1)$\,mm rather than the expected $(0, 0, L_0)$, which confirmed the frame offset. Three frame-transform variants were then evaluated on the validation split (Table~\ref{tab:res_pcc_frame}): the raw frame, a pure translation that maps the straight-pose tip to $(0, 0, L_0)$, and a translation combined with a $z$-axis flip. The flip-and-translation variant raised the reachable fraction from $51\%$ to $99.6\%$, but it reduced the mean forward residual by only $1.7$\,mm. Frame misalignment therefore accounts for the reachability artefact, but not for the residual.

\paragraph{Elongation diagnostic.}
A second hypothesis was that the arm elongates under pressurisation, which a single-segment constant-length PCC has no degree of freedom to represent. Fig~\ref{fig:res_pcc_elongation} plots the training-split tip norm against two actuation summaries: the sum of the three valve commands, as a proxy for total pressurisation, and the cross-chamber standard deviation of the commands, as a proxy for bending magnitude. The tip norm correlates with total pressurisation at Pearson $r = +0.737$, and with the bending proxy at only $r = +0.429$. The elongation signal therefore dominates the bending signal in this workspace, and the constant arc length $L$ assumed in Eq.~\eqref{eq:pcc_L} is unable to absorb it. This is the structural ceiling on the reachable-subset forward residual.

\paragraph{Interpretation.}
Taken together, the frame and elongation diagnostics indicate that the single-segment constant-length PCC can reach almost every target in the Dataset 2 workspace once the frame is corrected, but it cannot reproduce the pressure-induced elongation implied by the $r = +0.737$ correlation. The remaining ${\sim}22$\,mm mean residual on the reachable subset is therefore not a deficiency of the linear length-to-actuation fit but a ceiling imposed by the model class itself. This finding motivates the data-driven inverse-kinematics methods reported in the following sections, which do not commit to a fixed arc length and can therefore absorb the elongation coupling implicitly from the training data.

\subsection{Two-segment PCC on Dataset 1}
\label{sec:res_pcc_multi}

The two-segment PCC pipeline of Section~\ref{sec:pcc_multi} was calibrated on the $7{,}981$-frame training split of Dataset 1 and evaluated on the $11{,}956$-frame test split through the closed-loop protocol: each test target was passed through the SLSQP inverse, the linear length-to-pressure fit, and the forward kinematics of Eq.~\eqref{eq:pcc_multi_fk}, and the Euclidean distance to the target was recorded under the learned arbiter of Section~\ref{sec:jacobian}. The outcome is a clean negative result: the geometric inverse fits almost exactly, but the linear length-to-pressure calibration collapses and drives the closed-loop error above what even a small feed-forward network achieves on the same targets.

\paragraph{Geometric inverse is essentially exact.}
The SLSQP residual $\| \mathbf{x}^{*} - \mathbf{p}_{\mathrm{tip}}(\boldsymbol{\xi}) \|$ at convergence---measured \emph{before} the pressure calibration of Eq.~\eqref{eq:pcc_multi_linear} is applied---averaged $3.5 \times 10^{-3}$\,mm across the test set, with a $95$th percentile of $2.1 \times 10^{-4}$\,mm and a long-tail maximum of $10.2$\,mm attributable to the small fraction of targets at which SLSQP returned without fully converging. Every Dataset 1 tip target therefore admits a two-segment PCC configuration $\boldsymbol{\xi}$ that reproduces it to below the marker-noise floor. The warm-started SLSQP converged in a median of three iterations per target, and the per-target wall-clock time averaged $3$\,ms on the CPU.

\paragraph{Closed-loop error.}
Once the SLSQP configuration is mapped to chamber pressures by the linear fit and those pressures are passed through the arbiter forward map, the closed-loop position error rises to a mean of $51.22$\,mm with a $95$th percentile of $91.55$\,mm and a maximum of $165.63$\,mm (Table~\ref{tab:res_pcc_multi_vs_mlp}). The tip-only feed-forward network of Section~\ref{sec:mlp_tip}, evaluated on the same targets through the same arbiter, reaches a mean closed-loop error of $13.04$\,mm---a factor of $3.9$ smaller than PCC. Pressure-space metrics tell the same story: the two-segment PCC pipeline's coefficient of determination against the ground-truth chamber pressures is slightly negative ($R^{2} = -0.02$), so the linear length-to-pressure calibration explains no variance beyond predicting the per-chamber mean, whereas the feed-forward network achieves $R^{2} = +0.32$. Fig~\ref{fig:res_pcc_multi_compare} illustrates the contrast: the two distributions of closed-loop error are well separated on a logarithmic axis, and the per-chamber pressure residuals of the PCC pipeline sit consistently above those of the feed-forward network.

\paragraph{Failure mechanism.}
The combination of a near-zero geometric residual with a $51$\,mm closed-loop error is explained by the interaction between the multi-valued inverse and the linear length-to-pressure map. Dataset 1's six-chamber actuation is mapped into a three-dimensional tip position, so the inverse of Eq.~\eqref{eq:pcc_multi_fk} admits a continuous family of configurations for each target. The SLSQP solver picks out one member of this family per target, and the pressure calibration of Eq.~\eqref{eq:pcc_multi_linear} is fit by ordinary least squares on the training frames' $(\ell_{i}, V_{i})$ pairs. Because SLSQP can land on different solution branches for training frames whose tips are close together, the per-chamber scatter of $V_{i}$ at any given chamber length is large, and the least-squares fit regresses almost to the per-chamber mean ($R^{2} \approx 0$ per chamber). The calibrated pressures that the pipeline returns for a test target are therefore close to the training-set mean pressure rather than to the specific pressure that would have produced the target.

\paragraph{Interpretation.}
Together with the $22$\,mm structural ceiling on Dataset 2 (Section~\ref{sec:res_pcc_single}), this result shows that classical piecewise-constant-curvature inverse kinematics is not competitive with even a small feed-forward network on either of the two datasets used in this thesis. On Dataset 2, the constant-arc-length assumption is the bottleneck. On Dataset 1, that assumption holds well enough---the geometric inverse is essentially exact---but the multi-valued inverse combined with a linear pressure calibration destroys the connection between recovered chamber lengths and the actuation command that produced them. Two-segment PCC therefore serves in the remainder of this thesis as a classical baseline whose failure mode motivates the data-driven approaches of the following sections, rather than as a competitive method in its own right.

\paragraph{Dataset 2: clear improvement over PCC.}
On Dataset 2 the tip-only network reduced pressure MAE from $285.2$ to $150.1$ command units, a factor of $1.9$ improvement over the reachable-subset single-segment PCC baseline of Section~\ref{sec:res_pcc_single}. MAPE dropped from $62.0\%$ to $34.4\%$, NMAE from $11.4\%$ to $6.0\%$, and the coefficient of determination reached $R^{2} = 0.927$. Per-valve $R^{2}$ values of $0.960$, $0.955$, and $0.867$ (Fig~\ref{fig:res_mlp_pred_vs_actual}, top row) show that the network handles all three valves well, with valve $3$ slightly weaker than the other two. The network also predicted a command for every test target, whereas the PCC baseline had rejected $48.9\%$ of targets as unreachable.

\paragraph{Gap to the Ma et al.\ benchmark.}
Ma et al.\ \cite{ma2022bp} reported a pressure MAPE below $5\%$ on a similar three-chamber pneumatic arm, using a BP neural network trained on $216$ samples. The tip-only network of this thesis achieves $34.4\%$ MAPE on Dataset 2 with $12{,}650$ training samples, so the gap is not primarily a matter of dataset size. Contributing factors include the undocumented units and zero frame of Dataset 2 (already visible in the Section~\ref{sec:res_pcc_single} frame diagnostic) and the absence of any hysteresis term in a feed-forward architecture. The latter motivates the recurrent models reported in Section~\ref{sec:res_lstm}.

\paragraph{Dataset 1: low $R^{2}$ and an early validation plateau.}
On Dataset 1 the network achieved pressure MAE $20.13$, MAPE $71.3\%$, NMAE $20.1\%$, and $R^{2} = 0.323$ (Table~\ref{tab:res_mlp_tip}). Early stopping triggered at epoch $4$, indicating that the validation loss plateaued almost immediately and that further training would have risked overfitting. Per-chamber $R^{2}$ values (Fig~\ref{fig:res_mlp_pred_vs_actual}, bottom two rows) are markedly uneven: chambers $1, 3, 5, 6$ sit in the range $0.37$ to $0.60$, while chambers $2$ and $4$ are at or below chance ($R^{2} = -0.03$ and $0.04$ respectively). The combination of a low aggregate $R^{2}$, an early plateau, and two near-chance chambers is consistent with the two-segment inverse being under-determined from the tip alone: multiple pressure configurations can place the end-effector at the same tip, and the network can only learn the training-distribution-averaged configuration. Section~\ref{sec:res_mlp_tipmid} tests whether adding the mid-section marker resolves this ambiguity.

\paragraph{Inference time.}
Mean single-sample inference time was $0.08$\,ms on the CPU for both datasets. The feed-forward network is about an order of magnitude slower than the single-segment PCC baseline ($0.01$\,ms, Section~\ref{sec:res_pcc_single}), but it remains well under the $5$\,ms budget of a typical $200$\,Hz closed-loop controller.

\subsection{Tip-plus-mid ablation on Dataset 1}
\label{sec:res_mlp_tipmid}

Both variants were then evaluated on the identical set of $11{,}956$ joint-valid test frames.

\paragraph{Aggregate metrics.}
Adding the mid-marker input reduced pressure MAE from $20.13$ to $17.05$ command units (a $15\%$ reduction), MAPE from $71.3\%$ to $58.4\%$, and raised $R^{2}$ from $0.323$ to $0.437$ (Table~\ref{tab:res_mlp_tipmid}). The best validation epoch rose from $4$ in the tip-only configuration to $21$ in the tip-plus-mid configuration: with a richer input the network continued to extract useful signal for roughly five times as many epochs before the validation loss plateaued.

The improvement is substantial and stable, but $R^{2} = 0.437$ remains far below the value achieved on Dataset 2 ($0.927$), suggesting that even tip-plus-mid does not fully resolve the residual sources of error on Dataset 1. Other factors such as random-walk pressure excitation and hysteresis may contribute to what remains, and are examined in the recurrent results of Section~\ref{sec:res_lstm}.

\paragraph{Dataset 2: marginal improvement consistent with an IID distribution.}
On Dataset 2 the recurrent network reduced pressure MAE from $150.35$ to $143.08$ command units, a $4.8\%$ improvement, and lifted $R^{2}$ from $0.928$ to $0.931$. The finding is consistent with Section~\ref{sec:eda_ds2}'s conclusion that Dataset 2's rows behave as statistically independent samples rather than as a trajectory: the $10$-frame window carries almost no additional predictive signal beyond the current tip.

\paragraph{Dataset 1 tip-only: LSTM does not help.}
On Dataset 1 with the tip-only input, the recurrent network performed slightly worse than the feed-forward baseline. Pressure MAE rose from $20.15$ to $21.00$, and $R^{2}$ fell from $0.323$ to $0.283$. The best validation epoch of the recurrent run collapsed to $2$ at $K = 10$ and $1$ at $K = 20$, indicating that increasing the window length added parameters and over-fitting risk without giving the network signal it could generalise. The result extends the interpretation of Section~\ref{sec:res_mlp_tip}: if the tip-to-pressure inverse on a two-segment arm is fundamentally under-determined from the tip alone, then adding historical tip samples does not constrain the solution, and the recurrent network pays a parameter-count penalty for no benefit.

\paragraph{Dataset 1 tip-plus-mid: lowest Dataset 1 error reported in this thesis.}
When the mid-marker coordinates were added to the input, the recurrent network became the most accurate Dataset 1 configuration tested. Pressure MAE fell from $17.05$ (feed-forward tip-plus-mid) to $16.77$, $R^{2}$ rose from $0.437$ to $0.506$, and the per-frame RMSE mean fell from $21.75$ to $20.58$ command units. Fig~\ref{fig:res_lstm_ds1_all} shows the four Dataset 1 configurations side by side: the recurrent tip-plus-mid bar is the lowest. Temporal context therefore improves accuracy once the mid marker has disambiguated the per-segment configuration of the arm, even though the same temporal context does not help the tip-only case.

\paragraph{Interpretation.}
Taken together, the three configurations suggest that a recurrent architecture buys accuracy only when the single-frame input is informative enough for the trajectory of the past $K$ frames to carry a physically meaningful signal. On Dataset 1 with the tip alone, the current tip is under-determined and historical tips cannot resolve the internal ambiguity, so the recurrent network over-fits. Once the mid marker pins down the per-segment configuration, the past $K$ frames of that configuration carry motion-direction information that the feed-forward network cannot access, and the recurrent model finds an additional signal to exploit. The resulting $20.58$ command-unit per-frame RMSE on Dataset 1 is the lowest reported so far in this thesis, and is carried forward as the Dataset 1 reference in the Jacobian-learned and trajectory-tracking comparisons of Sections~\ref{sec:res_jacobian} and~\ref{sec:res_traj}.

\section{Jacobian-learned IK}
\label{sec:res_jacobian}

The Jacobian-learned inverse kinematics of Section~\ref{sec:jacobian} was evaluated in two complementary ways on each dataset: through the closed-loop position error and through the pressure-space error against the ground-truth commands. The two evaluations give opposite answers, and the contrast is the main finding of this section.

\paragraph{Forward-kinematics network quality.}
The forward-kinematics networks of Section~\ref{sec:jacobian} were trained first and frozen before any iteration was run. On Dataset 2 the network reached a test-set position MAE of $2.55$\,mm and a coefficient of determination of $R^{2} = 0.932$, with a best-epoch index of $38$. On Dataset 1 the same architecture, trained to map six-chamber pressures to base-relative tip positions, reached test MAE $12.97$\,mm and $R^{2} = 0.675$, with a best-epoch index of $2$---the same early-plateau behaviour observed for the Dataset 1 inverse-kinematics network in Section~\ref{sec:res_mlp_tip}.

\paragraph{Closed-loop position error.}
Table~\ref{tab:res_jacobian_pos} reports the per-target closed-loop position error for the feed-forward direct inverse kinematics of Section~\ref{sec:res_mlp_tip} and for the Jacobian-learned iteration under each of the three initialisation strategies of Section~\ref{sec:jacobian}. All errors are computed through the identical forward-kinematics arbiter. Dataset 2 was evaluated on the full $2{,}712$-frame test split; Dataset 1 was evaluated on a $1{,}500$-frame random sub-sample (seed $42$) because the Jacobian iteration is approximately nine hundred times slower than a forward pass of the direct network, and a full-sample run was not practical on the available workstation.

The warm-started Jacobian iteration reduced the mean closed-loop position error from $2.70$\,mm to $0.78$\,mm on Dataset 2 (a $71\%$ reduction) and from $12.88$\,mm to $0.71$\,mm on Dataset 1 (a $94\%$ reduction). It converged on $98.5\%$ of Dataset 2 targets within the $50$-iteration cap and on every Dataset 1 target in the sub-sample, taking $2.3$ iterations on average on Dataset 2 and $3.9$ on Dataset 1. Mean wall-clock time was approximately $46$\,ms per target on both datasets, against $0.05$\,ms for a single forward pass of the direct network.

\paragraph{Pressure-space trade-off.}
The closed-loop position error and the pressure-space error measure different things on a multi-valued inverse-kinematics problem, and the two give opposite answers in this experiment. Table~\ref{tab:res_jacobian_pressure} reports the mean absolute pressure error of the same four methods on each dataset. On Dataset 2 the direct feed-forward network's pressure MAE of $150.15$ command units is $25\%$ lower than the warm-started Jacobian's $200.92$; on Dataset 1 the gap is smaller but in the same direction ($20.06$ against $21.71$). The mechanism is the one anticipated by Fang et al.\ \cite{fang2022jacobian}: because multiple pressure configurations can produce the same tip, a direct-regression network minimises squared pressure error by regressing toward the average across valid solutions, whereas the Jacobian iteration commits to a single valid solution at inference and has no incentive to track the training-set pressure at that target. Position-space accuracy can be arbitrarily improved by continuing to iterate; pressure-space accuracy is bounded by the multi-valuedness of the inverse.

\paragraph{Initialisation ablation.}
The three initialisation strategies produced markedly different results on the two datasets. On Dataset 2 the zero initialisation failed on $22\%$ of targets, with a mean error of $4.79$\,mm and a maximum of $230$\,mm: the zero vector lies far outside the observed valve-command distribution $[0, 2500]$, and the local Jacobian at the starting point points in an unreliable direction for distant targets. The warm-start and nearest-neighbour strategies performed almost identically on closed-loop error, with the warm-start converging in fewer iterations on average ($2.3$ against $3.8$ on Dataset 2, $3.9$ against $5.0$ on Dataset 1). On Dataset 1 all three strategies converged for every target, because the random-walk command distribution of Section~\ref{sec:ds1} is centred near the zero vector and zero therefore lies inside the active workspace. Fig~\ref{fig:res_jacobian_convergence} shows the per-iteration position error of the warm-started iteration on ten Dataset 2 targets, selected by quantile of final error: nine of the ten drop below the $1$\,mm tolerance within about ten iterations and then oscillate at the floor of the DLS step, while the worst-quantile target plateaus above the tolerance and represents the $\sim$$1.5\%$ of Dataset 2 targets that did not converge within the iteration cap.

\paragraph{Runtime.}
At an average of $46$\,ms per target, the warm-started Jacobian iteration is approximately nine hundred times slower than the direct feed-forward network. Both methods remain well within the $50$\,ms budget of a $20$\,Hz closed-loop controller, but neither would meet a $1$\,kHz loop. The bulk of the Jacobian cost is the reverse-mode automatic differentiation of the forward model and the linear solve at each step (Eq.~\ref{eq:dls_step}); once the iterate reaches the $1$\,mm tolerance the solver exits, so the actual runtime scales roughly with the number of iterations rather than with the $50$-iteration cap.

\paragraph{Interpretation.}
Jacobian-learned inverse kinematics dominates the feed-forward direct inverse kinematics when position-space accuracy is the figure of merit: sub-millimetre mean error is achieved on both datasets with the warm-start protocol, at the cost of an order-of-magnitude increase in inference time. The feed-forward direct network is the better choice when pressure-space MAE is the target, because its least-squares fit regresses toward the multi-valued-inverse average. The two methods therefore play complementary roles in the remainder of this thesis: the warm-started Jacobian iteration is used as the primary inverse-kinematics method for the trajectory-tracking evaluation of Section~\ref{sec:res_traj}, and the feed-forward direct network remains the primary method for pressure-space reporting.

\section{Sim-to-real with PyElastica}
\label{sec:res_sim}

This section reports the outcome of the two-stage PyElastica experiment of Section~\ref{sec:sim}. Section~\ref{sec:res_sim_cal} gives the Young's-modulus calibration of the rod; Section~\ref{sec:res_sim_transfer} reports the sim-to-real transfer study on Dataset 2.

\subsection{Simulator calibration}
\label{sec:res_sim_cal}

The Young's-modulus scan described in Section~\ref{sec:sim_setup} selected $E = 8.86 \times 10^{4}$\,Pa, which lies inside the plausible range for soft silicone elastomers \cite{manti2016soft}. At this value the single-chamber actuation at $1.2$\,bar produced a tip-bending angle of $118.42^{\circ}$, a relative error of $0.35\%$ against the $118^{\circ}$ anchor, and the all-three-chamber actuation at the same pressure produced an axial elongation of $92.80\%$, a relative error of $9.2\%$ against the $85\%$ anchor. Both anchor errors fall below the $10\%$ acceptance threshold set in Section~\ref{sec:sim_setup}, so the calibration was accepted.

Fig~\ref{fig:res_sim_cal} shows the two anchors over the twenty-point logarithmic grid of Young's-modulus values. The bending anchor is monotone decreasing in $E$ across the grid, which gives a clean calibration signal and localises the best value to a narrow band near $10^{5}$\,Pa. The elongation anchor is almost flat between $9 \times 10^{4}$ and $10^{6}$\,Pa: the rest-length pre-stretch is prescribed by the coupling constant $k_L$ independently of $E$ (Eq.~\eqref{eq:sim_stretch}), so the residual eight-percentage-point gap above the $85\%$ target is set by the gravity sag of the free end rather than by the stiffness of the rod. Retuning $k_L$ toward $5.8 \times 10^{-6}$\,Pa$^{-1}$ would collapse the elongation residual below $2\%$, but the $9.2\%$ value already satisfies the acceptance criterion, so the coupling constants were not altered.

The pressure-MAE gap $\mathrm{MAE}_{B} - \mathrm{MAE}_{A}$ is within $\pm 1$ command unit at every budget and changes sign between the $10\%$ and $25\%$ budgets. On the Dataset 2 command range $[0, 2500]$ these differences lie inside the noise floor of repeated training runs with different initialisations. The sim pre-training therefore neither helps nor hurts on Dataset 2 at any real-data budget tested.

\paragraph{Strategy C fails catastrophically.}
A negative $R^{2}$ means the sim-only network predicts Dataset 2 valve commands \emph{worse} than a constant regressor that returns the per-chamber training mean. The failure is a direct consequence of the five-fold workspace mismatch described above: a network trained on tip coordinates in the range $[-241, 251]$\,mm is being asked to predict for test targets in the range $[-48, 56]$\,mm, a region in which almost none of its training data lives.

\paragraph{Real-data scaling dominates the story.}
Most of the gain from adding real data is captured in the jump from $10\%$ to $25\%$ of the Dataset 2 training split (MAE $185 \to 163$); from $25\%$ to $100\%$ the MAE improves only from $163$ to $150$. The feed-forward network is already close to what a $3 \to 3$ regressor can extract from Dataset 2 at about three thousand real training samples.

\paragraph{Interpretation.}
The Dataset 2 sim-to-real study is recorded here as a negative transfer-value result with a clean attributable cause. With the $720$-sample synthetic set generated by the I-Support-inspired rod of Section~\ref{sec:res_sim_cal}, pre-training provides no measurable benefit at any real-data budget tested, because the synthetic workspace lies almost entirely outside the Dataset 2 workspace. The $720$-sample budget is small by sim-to-real standards, and a larger synthetic set might help at low real-data budgets even without fixing the workspace gap. However, the flat Strategy-B-minus-Strategy-A curve at every budget suggests that the dominant bottleneck is geometric rather than sample-count-limited: an off-the-shelf Manti-inspired rod is simply not close enough to the Dataset 2 robot for its synthetic workspace to be useful as a pre-training prior. Chapter~\ref{chap:discussion} revisits this result alongside the two PCC negative findings of Section~\ref{sec:res_pcc_single}--\ref{sec:res_pcc_multi}.

\section{Trajectory tracking}
\label{sec:res_traj}

This section reports the unified trajectory-tracking evaluation of Section~\ref{sec:traj}. All methods trained in the preceding sections were scored on the same four reference trajectories (circle, square, figure-of-eight, and helical spiral), each sampled at $120$ target points, and sized to the safe radius of each dataset's workspace ($11.9$\,mm on Dataset 1 and $7.0$\,mm on Dataset 2). Every predicted pressure command was converted back to a tip position through the shared forward-kinematics arbiter of Section~\ref{sec:jacobian}, so differences between methods are pure inverse-kinematics-quality differences rather than arbiter differences.

\paragraph{Overall ranking.}
Table~\ref{tab:res_traj_overall} and Fig~\ref{fig:res_traj_bar} report the mean tracking error per method and per dataset, averaged over the four trajectories. The best Dataset 1 method is the warm-started Jacobian-learned inverse kinematics at $0.77$\,mm mean error---an order of magnitude below every other Dataset 1 method tested. The best Dataset 2 method is the Strategy-B sim-to-real MLP at $6.20$\,mm, followed by the tip-only feed-forward network at $7.40$\,mm, the tip-only recurrent network at $7.78$\,mm, the single-segment PCC baseline at $8.50$\,mm, and the warm-started Jacobian-learned inverse kinematics at $13.37$\,mm. The tip-plus-mid feed-forward network is the clear outlier on Dataset 1 at $19.08$\,mm, for reasons discussed below.

\paragraph{Dataset 1: Jacobian-learned IK dominates.}
On Dataset 1 the warm-started Jacobian-learned iteration hit the $1$\,mm DLS tolerance on every target of every trajectory, giving a mean error of $0.77$\,mm and a maximum of $1.00$\,mm across all four trajectories combined. This is the expected behaviour of a Jacobian iteration in a six-degrees-of-freedom over-parameterised system: the local Jacobian of the learned forward model is well-conditioned, the iterate has ample actuation redundancy to absorb each target, and the warm-start from the direct feed-forward network begins close to a feasible solution. The tip-only feed-forward network ($7.83$\,mm) and the tip-only recurrent network ($8.03$\,mm) are about an order of magnitude worse on the same trajectories, confirming that the advantage lies in the iteration itself rather than in the shared forward-kinematics arbiter.

\paragraph{Dataset 1: tip-plus-mid feed-forward network extrapolates out of distribution.}
The tip-plus-mid feed-forward network of Section~\ref{sec:res_mlp_tipmid}---the best feed-forward Dataset 1 method on the held-out per-sample test (pressure MAE $17.05$ command units)---is the worst trajectory-tracking method at $19.08$\,mm. The cause is the mid-marker substitution required by the evaluation protocol: a synthetic tip trajectory has no ground-truth mid-marker signal, so the mid channel was fixed at the training-distribution centroid for every target (Section~\ref{sec:traj}). The network was trained on a distribution in which the mid coordinates co-vary with the tip coordinates, so holding the mid channel constant forces the network onto an out-of-distribution slice of its input space at every non-centroid target. The result is a methodological warning rather than a judgement on model class: the tip-plus-mid network performs well when it sees realistic mid-marker inputs, and performs poorly when asked to generalise to a constant-mid input for which it was not trained.

\paragraph{Dataset 2: the sim-to-real MLP wins the small-workspace tracking.}
On Dataset 2 the Strategy-B sim-to-real MLP reached the lowest mean tracking error at $6.20$\,mm, against $7.40$\,mm for the tip-only real-only network. This is the opposite ordering from the per-sample pressure-space evaluation of Section~\ref{sec:res_sim_transfer}, in which Strategies A and B were indistinguishable. The $1.2$\,mm advantage is consistent across the circle, square, and figure-of-eight trajectories (Table~\ref{tab:res_traj_ds2_per_traj}) and is clearly separated from the noise. Two explanations are consistent with the data. First, the trajectory evaluation covers a small smooth subset of the workspace (safe radius $7$\,mm), whereas the pressure-space evaluation uses the full Dataset 2 test distribution including its edges; the sim prior may add a weak regularising effect in the interior even when it fails to help at the edges. Second, position-space error averages out the per-sample cancellations that pressure-space MAE sees directly, so two networks with nearly identical pressure-space statistics can still differ in closed-loop position error.

\paragraph{Dataset 2: Jacobian-learned IK diverges on the spiral.}
The warm-started Jacobian iteration, which wins Dataset 1, comes fifth on Dataset 2 with a mean of $13.37$\,mm. Table~\ref{tab:res_traj_ds2_per_traj} shows that the Dataset 2 spiral is responsible. The Jacobian iteration's mean error on the circle, square, and figure-of-eight trajectories sits at $6.84$--$7.38$\,mm, comparable with every other feed-forward method, but on the spiral it rises to $31.94$\,mm, with a maximum single-target error of $258.55$\,mm. The spiral rises from the bottom to the top of the workspace along the full $z$-half-extent, which takes the target sequence through points close to the edge of what the learned forward model can represent. On Dataset 2 the three-valve actuation leaves no redundancy to recover from a poor DLS update at those edge targets, and a single diverging run inflates the mean across all $120$ spiral samples. The feed-forward methods degrade more gracefully because a single forward pass cannot diverge.

\paragraph{Recurrent versus feed-forward tracking.}
On every trajectory of both datasets, the recurrent network's mean tracking error sits within one millimetre of the corresponding feed-forward network's, and is marginally worse on three of the four Dataset 2 trajectories. Temporal context therefore does not improve trajectory tracking at this trajectory scale, even in the configurations where it improved the per-sample evaluation of Section~\ref{sec:res_lstm}. The reference trajectories are smooth and slow, so the current tip already carries most of the information the past $K$ frames could add.

\paragraph{Pressure-command smoothness.}
The sum-of-squared discrete third differences of the pressure stream (Eq.~\ref{eq:traj_jerk}) varies by several orders of magnitude across methods. On Dataset 1 the LSTM produces by far the smoothest pressure streams ($\mathcal{J} \in [0.03, 0.30]$), followed by the feed-forward MLPs ($\mathcal{J} \in [1, 80]$), followed by the Jacobian-learned iteration ($\mathcal{J} \in [2 \times 10^{2}, 10^{3}]$). The low LSTM jerk is a direct consequence of its hidden-state memory, which acts as a first-order low-pass filter on the pressure output. The Jacobian iteration has higher jerk because each target's DLS loop begins from the warm-start pressure rather than from the previous target's pressure, so the pressure stream is not smooth across successive targets. On Dataset 2 the numerical values of $\mathcal{J}$ are inflated by the five-hundred-fold-larger valve-command scale, but the ordering of methods is otherwise preserved.

\paragraph{Inference time.}
Per-step inference times were approximately $0.01$\,ms for PCC, $0.2$\,ms for every feed-forward network (tip-only, tip-plus-mid, and Sim-to-real), $0.3$\,ms for the recurrent network, and $27$\,ms for the warm-started Jacobian-learned iteration. Every method remains well within a $50$\,ms budget appropriate for a $20$\,Hz closed-loop controller, but none would meet a $1$\,kHz control loop; the Jacobian iteration is the only method with meaningful latency at tracking rate.

\paragraph{Interpretation.}
Trajectory tracking separates the methods into two regimes. When the dataset provides enough actuation redundancy and a smooth arbiter forward model---Dataset 1, with six chamber pressures and a well-conditioned learned forward map---the Jacobian-learned iteration drives closed-loop tip error to the millimetre tolerance floor and dominates every other method tested. When the dataset is under-actuated relative to its target workspace---Dataset 2, with three valves and a small disc-shaped workspace---the Jacobian iteration can diverge at workspace edges and a feed-forward network with a sim-to-real prior is the best tracker. In both regimes the recurrent and tip-plus-mid variants that dominate per-sample evaluation add no measurable benefit to smooth trajectory tracking, and in the Dataset 1 tip-plus-mid case the mid-marker substitution used at evaluation time actively degrades the network's input distribution. These regime-specific conclusions are revisited in Chapter~\ref{chap:discussion}.
