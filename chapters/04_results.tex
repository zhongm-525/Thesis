\section{Data Exploration Findings}
\label{sec:eda_findings}

Four findings from the Section~\ref{sec:eda} exploratory analysis are load-bearing for the rest of the thesis; their derivations, per-recording tables and diagnostic figures are collected in Appendix~\ref{app:eda_detail}.

\paragraph{Dataset~1 integrity and preprocessing support.}
$23{,}936$ usable frames remain after joint mid/tip occlusion filtering ($0.27\%$ drop-out from $24{,}000$ raw; per-recording counts in Table~\ref{tab:app_eda_ds1_occlusion}). The base is effectively stationary within every recording once occluded frames are excluded, and the four seeds' base-relative tip clouds overlap within a $\sim$$12$\,mm radius---both findings justify the translation-only base subtraction of Section~\ref{sec:splits}.

\paragraph{Dataset~1 noise floor.}
Seed-0 repeatability across three trials, paired by nearest neighbour in pressure-command space, gives per-frame tip-std mean $2.29$\,mm (median $1.88$, $95$th $4.14$; distribution in Fig~\ref{fig:app_ds1_repeatability}). The $\sim$$2.3$\,mm mean is the Dataset~1 closed-loop noise floor: any lower reported error is noise-fitting, not IK accuracy.

\paragraph{Dataset~2 is IID, not a trajectory.}
$18{,}072$ rows, no missing or duplicate values, commands in $[0, 2500]$, tip in a $\sim$$100 \times 104 \times 64$\,mm disc (Section~\ref{sec:ds2}). The consecutive-row step-size ratio of $0.96$ in tip-space and $1.003$ in valve-command space (both indistinguishable from unity) show the rows behave as an IID sample, not a temporal sequence; Dataset~2 is therefore treated as IID throughout, with the $70/15/15$ order-preserving split retained as a precaution.

\paragraph{Dataset~2 actuation--pose non-degeneracy.}
Every valve--tip Pearson coefficient has magnitude above $0.09$, with the strongest entries consistent with three chambers at $120^{\circ}$ (Fig~\ref{fig:app_ds2_correlation}): valve~1 pulls $-Y, +Z$; valve~2 pulls $+X$; valve~3 pulls $-X$. The map is non-degenerate, the minimal precondition for an inverse to have structure to fit.

\section{PCC analytical baseline}
\label{sec:res_pcc}

The PCC baselines provide a classical reference and probe two failure modes of analytical modelling: a structural ceiling on Dataset~2 (single-segment) and a multi-valued-inverse calibration collapse on Dataset~1 (two-segment). Neither is competitive with a small feed-forward network; together they motivate the data-driven methods below.

\subsection{Single-segment PCC on Dataset 2}
\label{sec:res_pcc_single}

\paragraph{Calibration and headline.}
$L_0 = 67.24$\,mm ($1.05 \times \max \|\mathbf{p}\|$ on train); per-chamber slopes $\mathbf{a} \approx (31.7, 23.2, 18.9)$ and intercepts $\mathbf{b} \approx (1927, 1703, 1941)$ absorb chamber-specific gains. On the $51\%$ reachable subset, the forward residual mean is $22.67$\,mm, an order of magnitude above Dataset~1's marker noise and more than $2\times$ Ma et al.\ \cite{ma2022bp}'s $5\%$ MAPE scale. $48.9\%$ of targets fail the workspace condition; penalised full-set mean is $87.64$\,mm (Table~\ref{tab:res_pcc_single}; distribution in Fig~\ref{fig:res_pcc_error}).

\subsection{Two-segment PCC on Dataset 1}
\label{sec:res_pcc_multi}

Calibrated on Dataset~1's $7{,}981$-frame train and evaluated on $11{,}956$ test frames through the shared FK arbiter: the geometric inverse fits almost exactly but the linear length-to-pressure calibration collapses.

\paragraph{Geometric inverse is essentially exact.}
SLSQP residual (pre-calibration) averaged $3.5 \times 10^{-3}$\,mm across the test set, $95$th percentile $2.1 \times 10^{-4}$\,mm. Warm-started SLSQP: median $3$ iterations, $3$\,ms per target.

\paragraph{Closed-loop error.}
After the linear pressure calibration, the closed-loop error rises to $51.22$\,mm mean (max $165.63$; Table~\ref{tab:res_pcc_multi_vs_mlp})---$3.9\times$ the tip-only feed-forward network's $13.04$\,mm on the same targets. Pressure metrics confirm: PCC $R^{2} = -0.02$ (calibration explains no variance beyond per-chamber mean), network $R^{2} = +0.32$ (Fig~\ref{fig:res_pcc_multi_compare}).

\paragraph{Failure mechanism.}
Six-chamber actuation maps into a three-dimensional tip, so Eq.~\eqref{eq:pcc_multi_fk} admits a continuous configuration family per target. SLSQP picks one; different training frames land on different branches, so the per-chamber scatter of $(\ell_i, V_i)$ is large and the OLS calibration regresses to the per-chamber mean ($R^{2} \approx 0$ per chamber)---the returned pressures lie near the training-set mean, not near the target's true actuation.

\paragraph{Interpretation.}
Together with the Dataset~2 structural ceiling (Section~\ref{sec:res_pcc_single}), this shows PCC is not competitive on either dataset: constant-$L$ is the Dataset~2 bottleneck; the multi-valued inverse plus linear calibration is the Dataset~1 bottleneck. PCC is kept as a classical baseline, not as a competitive method.

\paragraph{Dataset 2.}
The network beats PCC by $1.9\times$ on pressure MAE and reaches $R^{2} = 0.927$ (Table~\ref{tab:res_mlp_tip}). Per-valve $R^{2}$ is $0.960 / 0.955 / 0.867$ (Fig~\ref{fig:res_mlp_pred_vs_actual}, top row), with valve~3 slightly weaker. Unlike PCC, it predicts every test target. The $34.4\%$ MAPE nonetheless sits well above Ma et al.'s $<5\%$ benchmark \cite{ma2022bp}; the gap is not sample-count (12{,}650 vs 216) but the undocumented units/zero frame of Dataset~2 and the absence of a hysteresis term---the latter motivates the recurrent models of Section~\ref{sec:res_lstm}.

\paragraph{Dataset 1.}
$R^{2} = 0.323$ with early stopping at epoch~$4$, and chambers~2 and 4 sit at or below chance ($R^{2} = -0.03$ and $0.04$; Fig~\ref{fig:res_mlp_pred_vs_actual}, bottom rows). The combination is consistent with the two-segment inverse being under-determined from the tip alone, so the network learns the training-distribution-averaged pressure; Section~\ref{sec:res_mlp_tipmid} tests whether the mid marker resolves this. Inference is $0.08$\,ms per target, well under a $200$\,Hz controller budget.

\subsection{Tip-plus-mid ablation on Dataset 1}
\label{sec:res_mlp_tipmid}

The tip-plus-mid network of Section~\ref{sec:mlp_tipmid} was trained on the same Dataset 1 training split as the tip-only variant of Section~\ref{sec:res_mlp_tip}, with the only change being the extension of the input to the six-dimensional vector $(x_{\mathrm{tip}}, y_{\mathrm{tip}}, z_{\mathrm{tip}}, x_{\mathrm{mid}}, y_{\mathrm{mid}}, z_{\mathrm{mid}})$. Both variants were then evaluated on the identical set of $11{,}956$ joint-valid test frames.

\paragraph{Aggregate.}
Mid-marker input cuts MAE $20.13 \to 17.05$ ($-15\%$) and lifts $R^{2}$ $0.323 \to 0.437$ (Table~\ref{tab:res_mlp_tipmid}); best epoch rises from $4$ to $21$, consistent with a richer input supplying useful signal over roughly five times as many training passes before plateau.

\section{Jacobian-learned IK}
\label{sec:res_jacobian}

Jacobian-IK was evaluated through closed-loop position error (primary) and pressure-space error (secondary); the two give opposite rankings.

\paragraph{FK arbiter quality.}
Dataset~2: MAE $2.55$\,mm, $R^{2} = 0.932$, best epoch $38$. Dataset~1: MAE $12.97$\,mm, $R^{2} = 0.675$, best epoch $2$ (same early plateau as Section~\ref{sec:res_mlp_tip}).

\paragraph{Closed-loop error.}
Dataset~2 evaluated on the full $2{,}712$-frame test split; Dataset~1 on a $1{,}500$-frame random sub-sample (seed $42$), because the iteration is $\sim$$900\times$ slower than a forward pass. Warm-started Jacobian cuts mean error $2.70 \to 0.78$\,mm on Dataset~2 ($-71\%$) and $12.88 \to 0.71$\,mm on Dataset~1 ($-94\%$); convergence $98.5\%$ and $100\%$ respectively, mean $2.3$/$3.9$ iterations, $46$\,ms per target against the MLP's $0.05$\,ms (Table~\ref{tab:res_jacobian_pos}).

\paragraph{Runtime and interpretation.}
$46$\,ms per target is $\sim$$900\times$ slower than the MLP but still under a $20$\,Hz controller budget; a $1$\,kHz loop would not be reachable. \section{Sim-to-real with PyElastica}
\label{sec:res_sim}

This section reports the outcome of the two-stage PyElastica experiment of Section~\ref{sec:sim}. Section~\ref{sec:res_sim_cal} gives the Young's-modulus calibration of the rod; Section~\ref{sec:res_sim_transfer} reports the sim-to-real transfer study on Dataset 2.

\subsection{Simulator calibration}
\label{sec:res_sim_cal}

The Young's-modulus scan described in Section~\ref{sec:sim_setup} selected $E = 8.86 \times 10^{4}$\,Pa, which lies inside the plausible range for soft silicone elastomers \cite{manti2016soft}. At this value the single-chamber actuation at $1.2$\,bar produced a tip-bending angle of $118.42^{\circ}$, a relative error of $0.35\%$ against the $118^{\circ}$ anchor, and the all-three-chamber actuation at the same pressure produced an axial elongation of $92.80\%$, a relative error of $9.2\%$ against the $85\%$ anchor. Both anchor errors fall below the $10\%$ acceptance threshold set in Section~\ref{sec:sim_setup}, so the calibration was accepted.

Fig~\ref{fig:res_sim_cal} shows the two anchors over the twenty-point logarithmic grid of Young's-modulus values. The bending anchor is monotone decreasing in $E$ across the grid, which gives a clean calibration signal and localises the best value to a narrow band near $10^{5}$\,Pa. The elongation anchor is almost flat between $9 \times 10^{4}$ and $10^{6}$\,Pa: the rest-length pre-stretch is prescribed by the coupling constant $k_L$ independently of $E$ (Eq.~\eqref{eq:sim_stretch}), so the residual eight-percentage-point gap above the $85\%$ target is set by the gravity sag of the free end rather than by the stiffness of the rod. Retuning $k_L$ toward $5.8 \times 10^{-6}$\,Pa$^{-1}$ would collapse the elongation residual below $2\%$, but the $9.2\%$ value already satisfies the acceptance criterion, so the coupling constants were not altered.

\paragraph{Dataset 2: Sim2Real MLP wins; Jacobian-IK diverges on the spiral.}

\paragraph{Recurrent and smoothness.}
LSTM tracking error sits within $1$\,mm of the corresponding feed-forward network on every trajectory; temporal context adds nothing at these smooth slow trajectories. \paragraph{Inference time.}
$0.01$\,ms (PCC), $0.2$\,ms (feed-forward), $0.3$\,ms (LSTM), $27$\,ms (warm-start Jacobian). All fit $20$\,Hz; none fit $1$\,kHz. Only Jacobian has meaningful tracking-rate latency.
