The first, Dataset 1, was released with the I-Support soft manipulator \cite{manti2016soft} and extended to a two-module configuration by Bianchi et al.\ \cite{bianchi2023learning}. \subsection{Dataset 1 --- I-Support (20\,Hz)}
\label{sec:ds1}

The I-Support platform is the AM variant (3D-printed TPU Shore 80A) \cite{centurelli2022closed, alessi2024pushing}. Two modules of $\sim$$205$\,mm rest length assemble into a $409$\,mm, $230.5$\,g arm \cite{bianchi2024softoss}. Three McKibben chambers per module sit at $120^{\circ}$, with chambers $1$--$3$ proximal and $4$--$6$ distal, and a $60^{\circ}$ inter-module offset that aligns chamber~$1$ and chamber~$5$ on a common lateral axis \cite{bianchi2024softoss}. Maximum safe chamber pressure $120$\,kPa with a dead zone below $\sim$$20$\,kPa \cite{alessi2024pushing, bianchi2024softoss}. Alessi et al.\ \cite{alessi2024pushing} Table~1 reports asymmetric transients ($0.2$\,s load, $0.56$\,s unload), corresponding to $\sim$$4$ and $\sim$$11$ frames at $20$\,Hz---a motivation for the recurrent baseline, since a single-frame regressor cannot represent the unload tail.

Nine reflective markers were attached in three triads: on the fixed base, on the mid-section between the two modules, and on the end-effector. A random-walk pressure sequence drove the six chambers at a sampling rate of 20\,Hz. Six recordings of 4{,}000 frames were provided, covering four random-number-generator seeds (0, 1, 2, 3), with the seed-0 trial repeated three times to quantify run-to-run repeatability. Each frame holds the three-dimensional positions of all nine markers in millimetres, together with the six chamber pressures in raw units where a value near 100 corresponds to 1\,bar. Marker occlusions were present in roughly 0.3\% of the raw frames. The mid- and tip-marker triads were occluded at the same frames, so the same subset remained usable for every input variant considered in this thesis. The base-relative tip distribution is shown in Fig~\ref{fig:ds1_workspace}.

\subsection{Dataset 2 --- three-valve pneumatic recording}
\label{sec:ds2}

Dataset 2 comprises 18{,}072 frames. Each frame contains three valve commands and a three-dimensional tip position. The valve commands are integer-valued within $[0, 2500]$. No calibration to physical pressure was provided with the release, so the commands were treated as an un-calibrated actuation coordinate. Tip positions were supplied in inches and were converted to millimetres (factor 25.4) before further processing.

After conversion, the tip coordinates spanned approximately $[-47.9, 53.8]$\,mm laterally and $[-27.5, 36.9]$\,mm axially, giving a shallow disc-shaped workspace roughly 100\,mm wide and 65\,mm deep (Fig~\ref{fig:ds2_workspace}). No missing values and no duplicate frames were present. The frames are ordered as recorded, and the original order was preserved at every preprocessing stage. The source robot is driven by three valves, but its number of segments, geometry, and physical parameters are not documented in the release. Dataset 2 was therefore treated as a black-box mapping from actuation commands to tip pose, and no geometric assumptions were made without direct verification.

\subsection{Data splits and preprocessing}
\label{sec:splits}

Dataset 1 was split at the seed level. The seed-1 and seed-2 recordings formed the training set. The seed-3 recording served as the validation set. The three repetitions of seed~0 constituted the test set. This arrangement keeps all repeated runs together, so intra-seed repeatability can be reported on the test metric as a physical noise floor for the IK error. Frames from the same seed were never mixed across splits. Dataset 2 was split chronologically in the ratio $70/15/15$, yielding 12{,}650 training, 2{,}710 validation, and 2{,}712 test frames. The split assignment and the within-segment temporal order were preserved throughout: Dataset~2 was not chronologically shuffled, and Dataset~1 sliding windows were not allowed to cross seed boundaries. Mini-batch shuffling during optimisation reorders the \emph{already-built} training windows at each epoch and does not affect split assignment or the within-window temporal order.

Tip and mid coordinates in Dataset 1 were expressed relative to the base triad. For each frame, the base position was taken as the mean of the three base markers and subtracted from the mid and tip means. This removes any static translation offset introduced by re-installing the robot between recordings. The correction is sufficient in the absence of rotational drift between sessions, and the assumption was checked in the exploratory analysis. Frames in which either the mid or the tip triad contained a missing marker were dropped jointly, leaving 23{,}936 usable frames across the six recordings. Joint dropping ensures that the tip-only and tip-plus-mid neural inverse-kinematics variants share an identical sample set.

Inputs and outputs were standardised to zero mean and unit variance. Scaler parameters were fitted on the training split only, with input and output scalers fitted independently. For the recurrent models, standardisation was applied before the sliding-window construction. Sliding windows were constrained to remain within a single seed in Dataset 1 and within a single split in Dataset 2, so no window crossed a recording or split boundary.

\section{Analytical Baseline: PCC Inverse Kinematics}

\subsection{Single-Segment PCC (Exp 1A, Dataset 2)}
\label{sec:pcc_single}

A single-segment, three-chamber piecewise-constant-curvature (PCC) model was used as an analytical inverse-kinematics baseline on Dataset 2. The PCC formulation assumes that the actuator centreline bends as a circular arc of constant curvature, with three fluidic chambers equally spaced at $120^{\circ}$ around the neutral axis and offset from it by a distance $d$ \cite{webster2010review}. The geometric inverse kinematics were taken from Ma et al.\ \cite{ma2022bp}, Eqs.\ 11--17, with three configuration variables: the azimuth $\varphi$ of the bending plane, the bending angle $\theta$, and the arc length $L$ of the centreline.

\paragraph{Geometry.}
Given a target tip position $(x, y, z)$ in the base-relative frame, with the straight-pose axis along $+z$, the configuration was computed as
\begin{align}
  \varphi &= \operatorname{atan2}(y, x), \label{eq:pcc_phi} \\
  \theta  &= \arccos\!\left(\frac{z^{2} - (x^{2} + y^{2})}{z^{2} + (x^{2} + y^{2})}\right), \label{eq:pcc_theta} \\
  L       &= z \, \theta / \sin\theta. \label{eq:pcc_L}
\end{align}
Targets with $z \le 0$ fall outside the single-segment PCC workspace and were flagged as unreachable. The straight-pose singularity ($x = y = 0$) was handled as a special case with $\theta = 0$, $\varphi$ arbitrary, and $L = z$. The configuration was then mapped to three chamber lengths $(\ell_1, \ell_2, \ell_3)$ by
\begin{equation}
  \ell_i = L - \theta \, d \, \cos(\alpha_i - \varphi), \qquad \alpha_i \in \{\pi/2,\ 7\pi/6,\ 11\pi/6\}, \label{eq:pcc_lengths}
\end{equation}
where $\alpha_i$ denotes the azimuth of chamber $i$.

\paragraph{Length-to-actuation map.}
Ma et al.\ \cite{ma2022bp} relate chamber pressure to chamber length through a hyper-elastic constitutive law whose material constants are robot-specific. The material and the physical meaning of the actuation commands in Dataset 2 are not documented, so a simpler data-driven map was used. Each valve command was modelled as a linear function of its chamber-length change,
\begin{equation}
  V_i = a_i \, (\ell_i - L_0) + b_i, \qquad i = 1, 2, 3, \label{eq:pcc_linear}
\end{equation}
where $L_0$ is the reference segment length. This form keeps the number of free parameters small (six coefficients in total) and remains interpretable: the slopes $a_i$ capture how strongly a chamber elongates per unit command, and the intercepts $b_i$ capture the straight-pose offset of the actuation. The chamber offset was fixed to $d = 12$\,mm, a representative value for the I-Support platform \cite{manti2016soft}, because the geometry of the Dataset 2 robot is not documented. The reference length was set to $L_0 = 1.05 \cdot \max_k \| \mathbf{p}_k \|$, where $\mathbf{p}_k$ denotes the $k$-th training tip position. The multiplier of $1.05$ places $L_0$ slightly above the recorded workspace, so that most training samples satisfy $\ell_i < L_0$ and the fitted slopes are well conditioned. The coefficients $(a_i, b_i)$ in Eq.~\eqref{eq:pcc_linear} were fitted by ordinary least squares on the training split, and training samples whose targets fell outside the PCC workspace were dropped from the fit.

At inference, each test tip position was passed through Eqs.~\eqref{eq:pcc_phi}--\eqref{eq:pcc_linear} to produce three predicted valve commands, or an unreachable flag when the target failed the workspace condition. Two groups of errors were reported. On the reachable subset, pressure-space metrics---mean squared error, mean absolute error, mean absolute percentage error on samples with $|V| > 50$, and normalised mean absolute error relative to the full command range---were computed against the ground-truth valve commands. A forward residual was also computed on the reachable subset by inverting Eq.~\eqref{eq:pcc_linear} to recover chamber lengths from the predicted commands, inverting Eq.~\eqref{eq:pcc_lengths} to recover the configuration $(\theta, \varphi, L)$, and finally applying the closed-form PCC forward kinematics to obtain a reconstructed tip position. The Euclidean distance between the reconstructed and the target tip position was reported as the forward residual. For the full test set, a penalised mean was also reported, in which unreachable samples were assigned an error equal to the workspace diameter estimated from the training tip positions. The penalised mean provides a conservative upper bound that can be compared against downstream methods without over-crediting PCC on its favourable subset.

\subsection{Two-Segment PCC (Exp 1B, Dataset 1)}
\label{sec:pcc_multi}

The I-Support manipulator consists of two PCC-like modules stacked in series, which the single-segment model of Section~\ref{sec:pcc_single} cannot represent. A two-segment extension was implemented following Sokolov et al.\ \cite{sokolov2024twoseg}, who compose two single-segment PCC transforms with a $60^{\circ}$ inter-segment offset that matches the I-Support geometry exactly. No closed-form inverse exists for the compound model, so the inverse was solved numerically as an optimisation problem, following the formulation of Keyvanara et al.\ \cite{keyvanara2023geometric}.

\paragraph{Forward kinematics.}
Each segment $s \in \{1, 2\}$ was parameterised by its bending angle $\theta_s$, bending-plane azimuth $\varphi_s$, and elongation $\Delta L_s$ from a nominal rest length $L_s^{(0)} = 205$\,mm \cite{manti2016soft}. The segment arc length was $L_s = L_s^{(0)} + \Delta L_s$. The local transform from the base of segment $s$ to its tip is a displacement
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{t}_s = \frac{L_s}{\theta_s} \begin{pmatrix} (1 - \cos\theta_s) \cos\varphi_s \\ (1 - \cos\theta_s) \sin\varphi_s \\ \sin\theta_s \end{pmatrix},
  \label{eq:pcc_multi_segment_tip}
\end{equation}
combined with a rotation $\mathbf{R}_s$ by $\theta_s$ about the axis $(-\sin\varphi_s,\, \cos\varphi_s,\, 0)$, which is the normal to the bending plane. The limit $\theta_s \to 0$ corresponds to a straight-pose translation by $L_s$ along $+z$.

The compound tip position was obtained by chaining the two segment transforms with a fixed rotation about the local $z$-axis between them, matching the I-Support inter-segment offset:
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{p}_{\mathrm{tip}} = \mathbf{t}_1 + \mathbf{R}_1 \, \mathbf{R}_z(\pi/3) \, \mathbf{t}_2, \label{eq:pcc_multi_fk}
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{R}_z(\pi/3)$ denotes the rotation matrix about $z$ by $60^{\circ}$.

Dataset 1's base-relative frame places the robot's rest pose along $-z$ (the arm hangs downward), whereas Eq.~\eqref{eq:pcc_multi_segment_tip} assumes a rest pose along $+z$. Target tip positions were therefore reflected across the $z$-axis before being passed to the inverse kinematics, and reconstructed tips were reflected back before error evaluation.

\paragraph{Inverse kinematics.}
The configuration $\boldsymbol{\xi} = (\theta_1, \varphi_1, \Delta L_1, \theta_2, \varphi_2, \Delta L_2)^{\top}$ is not uniquely determined by the three-dimensional tip, so the inverse was formulated as a box-constrained least-squares optimisation \cite{keyvanara2023geometric},
\begin{equation}
  \min_{\boldsymbol{\xi} \in \mathcal{B}} \; \left\| \mathbf{p}_{\mathrm{target}} - \mathbf{p}_{\mathrm{tip}}(\boldsymbol{\xi}) \right\|^{2}_{2}, \label{eq:pcc_multi_ik}
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{B}$ is the product of per-parameter bounds: $\theta_s \in [0, 2.5]$\,rad, $\varphi_s \in [-\pi, \pi]$, and $\Delta L_s \in [-50, 50]$\,mm per segment. The bending bound exceeds the single-chamber maximum of approximately 2\,rad reported for the I-Support platform \cite{manti2016soft}. The elongation bound was kept conservative to restrict the feasible configuration space to the expected operating envelope. Eq.~\eqref{eq:pcc_multi_ik} was solved by the SLSQP algorithm, with a slight-bend initialisation $(\theta_1, \theta_2) = (0.1,\, 0.1)$\,rad and all other parameters zero, chosen to avoid the $\theta = 0$ singularity of Eq.~\eqref{eq:pcc_multi_segment_tip}. Because the solver returns a single local optimum and the multi-valued inverse allows different initialisations to converge to different solution branches, within each recording the solver initialised frame $k$ from the solution of frame $k-1$, following the trajectory-continuity protocol of Keyvanara et al.\ \cite{keyvanara2023geometric}, and warm-starting was reset at recording boundaries.

For each segment, the configuration $(\theta_s, \varphi_s, L_s)$ was mapped to three chamber lengths by Eq.~\eqref{eq:pcc_lengths}, giving six chamber lengths $(\ell_1, \ldots, \ell_6)$ per frame. A per-chamber linear fit, analogous to Eq.~\eqref{eq:pcc_linear} but with six chambers,
\begin{equation}
  V_i = a_i \, (\ell_i - L_0) + b_i, \qquad i = 1, \ldots, 6, \label{eq:pcc_multi_linear}
\end{equation}
mapped chamber lengths to the recorded pressure commands. The reference length was set to $L_0 = 205$\,mm for every chamber. The coefficients $(a_i, b_i)$ were fitted on the training split by ordinary least squares, using chamber lengths recovered from the training targets through Eqs.~\eqref{eq:pcc_multi_ik}--\eqref{eq:pcc_lengths}.

Each test target was passed through Eqs.~\eqref{eq:pcc_multi_ik}--\eqref{eq:pcc_multi_linear} to produce a predicted six-chamber pressure vector. Two families of metrics were reported. First, pressure-space errors---mean absolute error, mean absolute percentage error, and the coefficient of determination $R^{2}$---were computed against the ground-truth commands. Second, a closed-loop position error was obtained by passing the predicted pressures back through the forward map (Eq.~\eqref{eq:pcc_multi_fk}) and measuring the Euclidean distance between the reconstructed and target tips. The geometric IK residual, defined as the SLSQP objective at convergence before the pressure calibration, was also recorded, so that the geometric-fit quality can be separated from the calibration quality.

\section{Feed-Forward Neural IK}
\label{sec:mlp}

The analytical PCC baselines of Sections~\ref{sec:pcc_single}--\ref{sec:pcc_multi} rely on a specific mechanical model of the robot and lose accuracy when its assumptions fail. A feed-forward neural network removes that reliance: the actuation--pose map is learned directly from data, at the cost of modelling capacity that must be regularised by the training set. The approach follows Giorelli et al.\ \cite{giorelli2015nn}, who compared a neural inverse-kinematics model against an analytical Jacobian method on a cable-driven soft arm. Two variants were implemented. A tip-only variant (Section~\ref{sec:mlp_tip}) used the base-relative tip position alone as input, representing the minimal-sensing case. A tip-plus-mid variant (Section~\ref{sec:mlp_tipmid}) augmented the input with the mid-section marker triad, to test whether shape information improves the inverse on the two-segment Dataset 1 robot.

\subsection{Tip-only MLP: architecture and training}
\label{sec:mlp_tip}

The network was a fully connected multilayer perceptron. The input was the base-relative tip position $(x, y, z)$ in millimetres (three features), and the output was the actuation command vector: three valve commands for Dataset 2 and six chamber pressures for Dataset 1. Four hidden layers were used, each followed by a ReLU activation, with no dropout and no weight decay. The hidden widths were set to $[128, 256, 256, 128]$ for Dataset 1 and $[64, 128, 128, 64]$ for Dataset 2. The larger network was allocated to Dataset 1 because its output space is higher-dimensional (six chambers rather than three) and its training split is larger. Fig~\ref{fig:mlp_arch} shows the topology schematically, and Table~\ref{tab:mlp_config} lists the per-variant dimensions and the resulting trainable-parameter counts.

Training used the mean squared error (MSE) loss on the z-score--standardised outputs, the standardisation having been fitted on the training split only. Parameter updates used the Adam optimiser with learning rate $10^{-3}$, batch size $64$, and no weight decay. Training was capped at $500$ epochs with early stopping on the validation loss, triggered after $30$ consecutive epochs without improvement. The checkpoint with the lowest validation loss was retained for all reported test metrics.

Test-set predictions were de-standardised through the stored output scaler before any error metric was computed. Four metrics were reported: mean absolute error (MAE) in the original actuation units, mean absolute percentage error (MAPE) computed on samples whose ground-truth pressure exceeded $1\%$ of the command-range maximum (to avoid divide-by-near-zero), normalised MAE (NMAE) expressed as a percentage of the command-range maximum, and the coefficient of determination $R^{2}$. Inference latency was measured as the wall-clock time to predict a single sample on the CPU.

\subsection{Tip-plus-mid ablation on Dataset 1}
\label{sec:mlp_tipmid}

The I-Support manipulator is underdetermined from its tip alone: the six-dimensional actuation space maps into a three-dimensional tip space, so multiple configurations can place the end-effector at the same point. A network that receives only the tip must collapse this ambiguity, which can succeed only when the training distribution steers consistently toward one solution branch. If the mid-section of the arm carries shape information that is independent of the tip, adding mid-marker coordinates to the input should reduce the ambiguity and lower the pressure-prediction error.

The hypothesis was tested by extending the tip-only input with the base-relative mean of the mid-section marker triad, producing a six-feature input $(x_{\mathrm{tip}}, y_{\mathrm{tip}}, z_{\mathrm{tip}}, x_{\mathrm{mid}}, y_{\mathrm{mid}}, z_{\mathrm{mid}})$. Every other design choice was kept identical to the tip-only network on Dataset 1: the same four-layer architecture with widths $[128, 256, 256, 128]$, the same ReLU activations, the same Adam optimiser and learning rate, the same batch size, the same early-stopping policy, and the same random seed. Only the first-layer fan-in changed, from three to six inputs.

Because the Dataset 1 loader drops frames where either the mid or the tip triad is occluded, the two variants were trained and tested on an identical set of joint-valid frames. Any per-sample difference in pressure error can therefore be attributed to the input-feature change rather than to a mismatch in the sample set.

The two variants were compared through three paired statistics computed on the common test set: the fraction of test frames on which the tip-plus-mid variant produced a lower per-sample pressure RMSE than the tip-only variant, the mean per-frame error difference (tip-only minus tip-plus-mid) with a bootstrap $95\%$ confidence interval, and the two-sided Wilcoxon signed-rank test on the paired per-frame errors. Aggregate MAE, MAPE, and $R^{2}$ were also reported for each variant.

\section{Temporal IK with LSTM}
\label{sec:lstm}

The feed-forward networks predict chamber pressures from a single-frame input. Pneumatic soft actuators, however, exhibit history-dependent behaviour: the same target tip can require different actuations depending on the arm's recent motion, because of viscoelastic creep in the silicone and flow dynamics in the pneumatic lines. A recurrent model that sees a short window of recent frames can, in principle, condition its prediction on this history. Long short-term memory (LSTM) networks \cite{hochreiter1997lstm} were used here as a standard choice for sequence-to-scalar regression.

Dataset 1 was the primary target of this experiment, since its recordings are continuous $20$\,Hz random-walk trajectories. Dataset 2 was also evaluated for completeness, although the exploratory analysis indicated that its rows do not carry meaningful temporal structure. On Dataset 2 the recurrent model therefore serves more as a negative control than as an expected improvement over the MLP.

\paragraph{Sliding-window construction.}
Let $K$ denote the window length in frames. Each training example was a contiguous window of $K$ frames of the same input features used by the feed-forward networks (tip-only, or tip-plus-mid for the Dataset 1 ablation), with the actuation command at the $K$-th (last) frame taken as its label. Windows were constrained to remain within a single recording in Dataset 1 and within a single split in Dataset 2, so that no window crossed a recording boundary or the train/validation/test boundary. The z-score standardisation was applied to the flat frame data before windowing, so that the per-feature mean and variance stayed identical to those of the corresponding feed-forward run. The first $K-1$ frames of each recording or split therefore had no label and were dropped. The same truncation was applied to the validation and test sets to keep inputs and labels aligned.

\paragraph{Architecture.}
Two LSTM layers \cite{hochreiter1997lstm} were stacked on the windowed input, with $128$ hidden units per layer and no recurrent dropout. The hidden state at the last time step of the second layer was projected to the actuation space by a single linear head. Input and output dimensions matched the feed-forward setup: $3 \to 3$ for the Dataset 2 variant, $3 \to 6$ for the Dataset 1 tip-only variant, and $6 \to 6$ for the Dataset 1 tip-plus-mid variant. The hidden width and depth were kept identical across the three configurations. Fig~\ref{fig:lstm_arch} shows the topology schematically, and Table~\ref{tab:lstm_config} lists the per-variant dimensions and trainable-parameter counts.

\section{Jacobian-Learned IK}
\label{sec:jacobian}

Direct regression from tip to pressure collapses multiple valid solution branches into a single averaged output, which lowers the reported mean pressure error but can drive the tip away from the target when the prediction is fed back to the forward map. A Jacobian-based alternative avoids the averaging by learning only the forward map (pressure $\to$ tip position) and inverting it iteratively at run time, so each target commits to a single solution near its initialisation rather than blending several solution branches. The approach follows Fang et al.\ \cite{fang2022jacobian}, who proposed this scheme as an efficient IK solver for soft robots with sim-to-real transfer.

\paragraph{Forward-kinematics network.}
A feed-forward network $f_{\theta}: \mathbb{R}^{n_p} \to \mathbb{R}^{3}$ was trained to predict the base-relative tip position from the actuation command, where $n_p = 3$ for Dataset 2 and $n_p = 6$ for Dataset 1. The architecture (four hidden layers with ReLU activations), hidden widths, and training protocol were inherited unchanged from the feed-forward inverse-kinematics networks of Section~\ref{sec:mlp_tip}, with only the input and output roles swapped. Table~\ref{tab:fk_config} lists the per-dataset dimensions and parameter counts. The network was trained in isolation and its weights were frozen for the entire inverse-kinematics iteration. Joint training of the forward and inverse models would collapse the benefit of the two-stage design \cite{fang2022jacobian}.

\paragraph{Iterative inverse.}
Given a target tip position $\mathbf{x}^{*} \in \mathbb{R}^{3}$ and a current pressure estimate $\mathbf{p}_k \in \mathbb{R}^{n_p}$, the iterate was updated by
\begin{align}
  \mathbf{x}_k            &= f_{\theta}(\mathbf{p}_k), \label{eq:jac_forward} \\
  \mathbf{e}_k            &= \mathbf{x}^{*} - \mathbf{x}_k, \label{eq:jac_err} \\
  \mathbf{J}_k            &= \left.\frac{\partial f_{\theta}}{\partial \mathbf{p}}\right|_{\mathbf{p}_k}, \label{eq:jac_def} \\
  \Delta \mathbf{p}_k     &= \mathbf{J}_k^{\top} \bigl( \mathbf{J}_k \mathbf{J}_k^{\top} + \lambda^{2} \mathbf{I}_{3} \bigr)^{-1} \mathbf{e}_k, \label{eq:dls_step} \\
  \mathbf{p}_{k+1}        &= \mathbf{p}_k + \alpha \, \Delta \mathbf{p}_k. \label{eq:dls_update}
\end{align}
Equation~\eqref{eq:dls_step} is the damped least-squares (DLS) step written in its dual form, which is numerically stabler than the primal form when $n_p \ge 3$. The damping term $\lambda^{2} \mathbf{I}_{3}$ regularises the update near configurations where $\mathbf{J}_k$ is rank-deficient, at the cost of a slower step in well-conditioned regions. The step size $\alpha$ scales the Newton-like direction and stabilises iteration when $f_{\theta}$ is strongly nonlinear. The Jacobian $\mathbf{J}_k$ of size $3 \times n_p$ was computed by reverse-mode automatic differentiation of the frozen FK network at each iterate. The iteration was carried out in the standardised input/output space of the FK network, but convergence and divergence were evaluated in physical millimetres so that the reported tolerance is directly comparable to the error metrics of the other baselines.

The damping coefficient was set to $\lambda = 0.05$, the step size to $\alpha = 0.5$, the convergence tolerance to $1$\,mm on the Euclidean tip error, the iteration cap to $50$, and the divergence criterion to a $5$-fold increase of the current error over the initial error. Algorithm~\ref{alg:jacobian_ik} summarises the full procedure.

\begin{algorithm}[ht]
\caption{Jacobian-learned inverse kinematics for one target.}
\label{alg:jacobian_ik}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require Trained FK $f_{\theta}$; target $\mathbf{x}^{*}$; initial pressure $\mathbf{p}_0$; parameters $\alpha$, $\lambda$, tolerance $\tau$, maximum iterations $K$, divergence factor $\rho$.
\State $\mathbf{p} \gets \mathbf{p}_0$; \quad $e_0 \gets \| \mathbf{x}^{*} - f_{\theta}(\mathbf{p}_0) \|$
\For{$k = 0, 1, \ldots, K - 1$}
  \State $\mathbf{x}_k \gets f_{\theta}(\mathbf{p})$
  \State $\mathbf{e}_k \gets \mathbf{x}^{*} - \mathbf{x}_k$
  \If{$\| \mathbf{e}_k \| < \tau$}
    \State \Return $\mathbf{p}$ (converged)
  \EndIf
  \If{$\| \mathbf{e}_k \| > \rho \cdot e_0$}
    \State \Return $\mathbf{p}$ (diverged)
  \EndIf
  \State $\mathbf{J}_k \gets \partial f_{\theta} / \partial \mathbf{p}$ at $\mathbf{p}$
  \State $\Delta \mathbf{p} \gets \mathbf{J}_k^{\top} (\mathbf{J}_k \mathbf{J}_k^{\top} + \lambda^{2} \mathbf{I}_3)^{-1} \mathbf{e}_k$
  \State $\mathbf{p} \gets \mathbf{p} + \alpha \, \Delta \mathbf{p}$
\EndFor
\State \Return $\mathbf{p}$ (did not converge)
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\paragraph{Initialisation.}
The choice of initial pressure $\mathbf{p}_0$ influences both the convergence rate and the solution branch to which the iterate commits. Three strategies were compared. The \emph{zero} strategy sets $\mathbf{p}_0 = \mathbf{0}$ and provides a dataset-agnostic baseline. The \emph{MLP warm-start} strategy takes $\mathbf{p}_0$ from the prediction of the direct-IK feed-forward network, so the iteration begins close to the plausible solution already identified by the regressor. The \emph{nearest-neighbour} strategy uses the ground-truth pressure of the training frame whose recorded tip position is closest to the test target in Euclidean distance. All three strategies were evaluated with identical values of $\alpha$, $\lambda$, tolerance, and iteration cap. The MLP warm-start was adopted as the primary configuration for the headline comparison against the other baselines.

Two classes of metrics were reported. The primary metric is the \emph{closed-loop position error}, defined as the Euclidean distance $\| \mathbf{x}^{*} - f_{\theta}(\mathbf{p}_{\mathrm{final}}) \|$ in millimetres between the target and the forward-evaluated prediction. It measures how well the predicted pressures drive the (learned) robot toward the target. The secondary metrics are the pressure-space errors against the ground-truth commands (MAE, MAPE, $R^{2}$). On a multi-valued inverse-kinematics problem the two classes need not agree, and both are reported so that the trade-off can be examined in Chapter~\ref{chap:results}. Convergence statistics---the fraction of targets that reached the $1$\,mm tolerance, the mean and $95$th-percentile iteration count, and the wall-clock time per target---were also recorded, so that accuracy and runtime can be compared head-to-head against the feed-forward baselines.

\section{Simulation and Sim-to-Real with PyElastica}
\label{sec:sim}

Dataset 2's source robot has no documented physical parameters and no simulator of its own. A plausible-parameter simulator can still be useful as a prior for sim-to-real experiments, provided that its limitations are acknowledged. An I-Support--inspired single-module Cosserat rod was therefore built in PyElastica \cite{gazzola2018pyelastica}, with the geometric and material values of Manti et al.\ \cite{manti2016soft} as a starting point.

\subsection{Simulator setup}
\label{sec:sim_setup}

\paragraph{Cosserat rod and geometry.}
A single straight Cosserat rod of $50$ elements was instantiated with nominal rest length $L_0 = 205$\,mm and cross-sectional radius $r = 30$\,mm. Material parameters followed Manti et al.\ \cite{manti2016soft}: silicone density $\rho = 1100$\,kg\,m$^{-3}$ and Poisson's ratio $\nu = 0.5$, yielding a shear modulus $G = E / 3$. Young's modulus $E$ was left as a free parameter to be calibrated. Gravity of $9.81$\,m\,s$^{-2}$ acted along $-z$. One end of the rod was fixed and the opposite end was free, matching the downward-hanging mounting of the physical I-Support arm. Linear analytical damping was applied to absorb transient oscillations, and the rod was integrated to static equilibrium with a position-Verlet stepper at time step $\Delta t = 10^{-5}$\,s over a total integration time of $T = 3.0$\,s.

\paragraph{Pneumatic actuation.}
Chamber $i \in \{0, 1, 2\}$ occupies the azimuth $\theta_i = 2 \pi i / 3$ around the rod axis. Chamber pressure $P_i$ in pascals was mapped to rod deformation through two effects. The first was a bending moment of magnitude
\begin{equation}
  M_i = k_m \, P_i, \qquad k_m = 1.5 \times 10^{-5}\,\mathrm{N\,m\,Pa}^{-1}, \label{eq:sim_moment}
\end{equation}
applied uniformly along the rod about the axis $\mathbf{a}_i = (-\sin\theta_i,\, \cos\theta_i,\, 0)$, which is perpendicular to the chamber's radial offset. Because a pressurised chamber elongates and places its side on the outside of the curve, the moment is oriented so that the rod bends away from the active chamber. The second effect was a uniform elongation of the rod's reference configuration, proportional to the mean chamber pressure $\bar{P} = \tfrac{1}{3}(P_1 + P_2 + P_3)$:
\begin{equation}
  L_{\mathrm{rest}}(\mathbf{P}) = L_0 \bigl( 1 + k_L \bar{P} \bigr), \qquad k_L = 7.5 \times 10^{-6}\,\mathrm{Pa}^{-1}. \label{eq:sim_stretch}
\end{equation}
Representing elongation as a change of the rest configuration, rather than as an axial tip force, avoids artificially stiffening the rod against the bending moments of Eq.~\eqref{eq:sim_moment}. This decoupling allows the two I-Support behavioural anchors---bending at a single chamber and pure elongation at all three chambers---to be matched by independent choices of $k_m$ and $k_L$. The coupling constants were fixed manually at the values above so that both anchors remained reachable within the feasible range $E \in [10^{4}, 10^{6}]$\,Pa. Only $E$ was calibrated. Table~\ref{tab:sim_params} lists the full set of simulator parameters, and Fig~\ref{fig:sim_rod} visualises the calibrated rod under four canonical actuations.

\paragraph{Young's modulus calibration.}
Young's modulus was calibrated against two behavioural anchors reported for the I-Support platform \cite{manti2016soft}: a single-chamber actuation at $1.2$\,bar should produce a tip-bending angle of $118^{\circ}$, and an all-three-chamber actuation at the same pressure should produce an axial elongation of $85\%$. A grid of $20$ candidate values of $E$ was defined on a logarithmic scale between $10^{4}$\,Pa and $10^{6}$\,Pa. For each candidate, both anchor simulations were run to equilibrium, and the tip-bending angle $\phi(E)$ and elongation fraction $\epsilon(E)$ were measured from the resulting node positions. The calibration score was the sum of squared relative errors,
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L}(E) = \left( \frac{\phi(E) - 118^{\circ}}{118^{\circ}} \right)^{2} + \left( \frac{\epsilon(E) - 0.85}{0.85} \right)^{2}, \label{eq:sim_cal}
\end{equation}
and the value that minimised $\mathcal{L}$ was retained. The calibration was accepted only if the relative error on each anchor was below $10\%$. The calibrated Young's modulus and its anchor errors are reported in Chapter~\ref{chap:results}.

\subsection{Sim-to-real transfer protocol}
\label{sec:sim_transfer}

The calibrated rod of Section~\ref{sec:sim_setup} was used as a source of synthetic $(\mathbf{P}, \mathbf{x})$ training data, which was then combined with Dataset 2 under three training strategies. The aim of the experiment was to quantify the transfer value of the simulator prior at several real-data budgets, rather than to produce a best-performing model on Dataset 2.

\paragraph{Synthetic data generation.}
Chamber-pressure triples $(P_1, P_2, P_3)$ were sampled independently and uniformly in $[0, 1.2]$\,bar with a fixed random seed of $42$. For each triple, the calibrated rod was run to static equilibrium under the settings of Table~\ref{tab:sim_params} and its tip position was recorded. A total of $800$ $(\mathbf{P}, \mathbf{x})$ pairs were generated and split into $720$ training and $80$ validation samples, with tip positions in millimetres. The synthetic chamber pressures were rescaled from pascals to the raw command range of Dataset 2, so that the synthetic output scale matched the target-domain scale. The sample count was capped at $800$ rather than the originally planned tens of thousands because a fully settled simulation at the calibrated settings takes several seconds of wall-clock time per sample, and a larger budget was not feasible on the available workstation.

\paragraph{Training strategies.}
Three data-combination strategies were compared (Table~\ref{tab:sim_strategies}). All three used the same feed-forward network as the tip-only inverse-kinematics variant on Dataset 2: a four-hidden-layer MLP with widths $[64, 128, 128, 64]$, ReLU activations, an MSE loss on z-score--standardised inputs and outputs, Adam with batch size $64$, and the standard early-stopping policy unless stated otherwise.

\emph{Strategy A (real-only)}: trained on a temporally ordered prefix of the Dataset~2 training split, with scalers fitted on that prefix. \emph{Strategy B (sim-pretrain + real fine-tune)}: pretrained on the $720$-sample synthetic set for up to $100$ epochs at $10^{-3}$, then fine-tuned on Strategy~A's real subset at $10^{-4}$ with the standard early-stopping policy. Scalers were fitted on the real subset and applied to both phases so the normalisation is shared. \emph{Strategy C (sim-only)}: trained on synthetic data only at $10^{-3}$, with scalers fitted on synthetic data, evaluated on the Dataset~2 test set without adjustment.

Strategy C was run once, since it uses no real data. All trained networks were evaluated on the full Dataset 2 test split, with predictions de-standardised before the error metrics were computed. The four metrics (MAE, MAPE, NMAE, $R^{2}$) were reported for every strategy and budget combination, so that the effect of the simulator prior can be read as the gap between Strategies A and B at each budget, and the sim-only transfer quality can be read from Strategy C.

\section{Trajectory Tracking Evaluation}
\label{sec:traj}

The per-sample evaluations of Sections~\ref{sec:pcc_single}--\ref{sec:sim_transfer} report errors on the held-out test sets, in which targets are drawn from the same distribution as the training data. A second, complementary evaluation mode was added so that every method could be compared on smooth reference trajectories of the kind a soft-robot controller might have to follow in practice. The trajectory-tracking experiment applied the same protocol---the same target shapes, the same workspace sizing, the same arbiter forward model, and the same metrics---to every trained inverse-kinematics method on each dataset.

\paragraph{Reference trajectories.}
Four reference trajectories were generated at $120$ equally spaced target points each. A \emph{circle} and a \emph{square} were drawn in the $XY$ plane of the robot's workspace, with the four corners of the square placed on sampling points so that the high-curvature corner segments were always represented. A \emph{figure-of-eight} was generated as the lemniscate of Gerono,
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{x}(t) = \bigl( r \sin t,\; r \sin t \cos t,\; 0 \bigr), \qquad t \in [0, 2\pi), \label{eq:traj_fig8}
\end{equation}
which passes through the workspace centre twice per period and therefore probes motion in both directions near the origin. A \emph{helical spiral} of two turns about the $z$-axis was added as the hardest trajectory: its pitch was set to the full $z$-extent of the workspace, so the target rises from the bottom of the workspace to the top while tracing a circle. Unlike the three planar trajectories, the spiral forces every method to predict axial actuation in addition to bending. The four trajectories were sized to lie inside the observed workspace of each dataset. Workspace bounds were fitted from the union of the training and validation tip positions: the centre was taken as the midpoint of the $[2\%, 98\%]$ interquantile range along each axis, and a \emph{safe radius} was defined as the smallest per-axis half-extent of that interquantile range, shrunk by a factor of $0.45$ so that each trajectory stayed safely inside the observed workspace. The circle, square, and figure-of-eight used the safe radius. The spiral used $0.8 \times$ the safe radius in the horizontal plane and the full $z$-half-extent for its pitch. The same sizing rule was applied to both datasets, so that the resulting trajectories are interpretable in common workspace-normalised terms even though their absolute sizes differ.

\paragraph{Arbiter forward model.}
Each method under evaluation returns a sequence of pressure commands $\{\mathbf{p}_k\}_{k=1}^{120}$ from the target sequence $\{\mathbf{x}^{*}_k\}_{k=1}^{120}$. Pressure commands were converted back to achieved tip positions for error measurement by the trained forward-kinematics network $f_{\theta}$ of Section~\ref{sec:jacobian}: the achieved tip at target $k$ was taken to be $\hat{\mathbf{x}}_k = f_{\theta}(\mathbf{p}_k)$. Using a single forward model as an arbiter for every method ensures that any difference in tracking error reflects the quality of the predicted pressures, not a difference in how those pressures are mapped back to positions. The forward-kinematics network is not a perfect physical model of the robot, but it is the same physical model for every method, which is the property that the comparison requires.

Every inverse-kinematics method trained above was included where a trained checkpoint existed. On Dataset 1 the set comprised the tip-only and tip-plus-mid feed-forward networks, the two LSTM variants, and the Jacobian-learned inverse kinematics with MLP warm-start. On Dataset 2 the set comprised the single-segment PCC baseline, the tip-only feed-forward network, the LSTM, the Jacobian-learned inverse kinematics, and the sim-to-real fine-tuned network (Strategy~B at the full real-data budget). The feed-forward checkpoint used by the Jacobian warm-start was frozen at the same weights, so the initialisation was identical across experiments.

\paragraph{Handling of the mid-marker input.}
The tip-plus-mid feed-forward network takes a six-dimensional input that pairs the tip and mid-section positions recorded in Dataset 1. A smooth reference trajectory is defined only in tip-space, so no ground-truth mid-marker signal exists for the generated targets. The mid channel was therefore substituted with the centroid of the training mid-marker distribution, held constant across the trajectory. This substitution allows the tip-plus-mid network to be scored on the same trajectories as the other methods, at the cost of forcing it onto a mid-channel value that is out-of-distribution for any non-centroid target. The implications of this choice are discussed with the results in Chapter~\ref{chap:results}.

Six quantities were recorded for each combination of method, dataset, and trajectory. The tracking-error distribution was summarised by the mean, the maximum, the $95$th percentile, and the root-mean-square of the per-target Euclidean distance $\| \mathbf{x}^{*}_k - \hat{\mathbf{x}}_k \|$ in millimetres. The smoothness of the pressure-command stream was summarised by the sum of squared discrete third differences,
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{J} = \sum_{k} \bigl\| \mathbf{p}_{k+3} - 3 \mathbf{p}_{k+2} + 3 \mathbf{p}_{k+1} - \mathbf{p}_k \bigr\|^{2}, \label{eq:traj_jerk}
\end{equation}
which approximates the integral of squared jerk and penalises methods whose predicted pressure sequences contain rapid oscillations. Finally, the per-target wall-clock inference time was recorded in milliseconds so that the runtime costs of the different methods can be compared at the tracking rate.

\section{Training Setup and Evaluation}

\subsection{Hardware, software, and reproducibility}
\label{sec:reproducibility}

All training and evaluation reported in this thesis were carried out on a single workstation running Linux. The hardware was an Intel Core i7-13650HX CPU with $16$\,GB of RAM and an NVIDIA GeForce RTX 4060 Laptop GPU. The software stack comprised Python $3.11$, PyTorch $2.11$ with CUDA $12.6$, NumPy, pandas, scikit-learn, SciPy, and PyElastica \cite{gazzola2018pyelastica}. Scikit-learn provided the z-score scalers and the nonparametric paired-test utilities. SciPy provided the SLSQP solver used by the two-segment PCC inverse kinematics. PyElastica provided the Cosserat rod integrator used by the I-Support simulator.

A fixed random seed of $42$ governed all stochastic operations, including scaler fitting order, mini-batch shuffling within a single Dataset 1 seed, and weight initialisation. The PyTorch cuDNN backend was set to deterministic mode with auto-benchmarking disabled, so that GPU kernel selection did not introduce run-to-run variation. Fitted input and output scalers were stored inside the model checkpoint metadata, so inference could be reproduced without recomputing dataset statistics. Each checkpoint also recorded the configuration file, the git commit hash, and the timestamp of the training run, so any reported number can be traced back to the exact code state that produced it.

\subsection{Hyperparameter selection}
\label{sec:hyperparam}

Hyperparameter selection followed a single rule: the test split of each dataset was held out until all modelling choices had been fixed. The validation-split loss was the only criterion used for model selection within a training run (through early stopping) and for any cross-configuration selection where one was performed.

For the feed-forward networks, only the hidden-layer depth and widths were selected, once per dataset, on short pilot runs driven by the validation loss. Learning rate, batch size, and optimiser were fixed at values commonly reported as robust defaults for small regression networks and were not swept. For the recurrent networks, the window length $K$ was swept over $\{5, 10, 20\}$ and the setting that minimised the validation loss on each dataset was retained for the headline comparison. The hidden width and depth were fixed at $128$ units and two layers respectively. For the Jacobian-learned inverse kinematics, the damping coefficient $\lambda$, the step size $\alpha$, and the convergence tolerance $\tau$ were set at reference values drawn from Fang et al.\ \cite{fang2022jacobian} with no further tuning. For the PCC baselines, the reference length $L_0$ of the single-segment model was scanned on a uniform grid over the training tip-norm range, and the SLSQP bounds of the two-segment model were fixed at physically plausible values from the I-Support specification. For the sim-to-real experiment, the pre-training and fine-tuning learning rates were chosen a priori at $10^{-3}$ and $10^{-4}$ respectively, following the common heuristic of reducing the rate by an order of magnitude for fine-tuning.

No hyperparameter was ever chosen on the basis of the test split. The test metrics reported in Chapter~\ref{chap:results} are therefore held-out generalisation estimates, not selection criteria.

\subsection{Metrics}
\label{sec:metrics}

Three families of metrics were used across the experiments: pressure-space errors (for methods whose direct output is an actuation command), position-space errors (for closed-loop evaluation through a forward model), and auxiliary metrics for smoothness and runtime. All pressure-space metrics are reported in the native actuation unit of each dataset: raw units in $[0, 100]$ for Dataset 1, where a value of approximately $100$ corresponds to $1$\,bar, and raw units in $[0, 2500]$ for Dataset 2. Note that $V_{\max} = 100$ used below is the \emph{empirical maximum recorded command} for Dataset~1 and matches the $\sim\!1$\,bar scale. The $1.2$\,bar hardware safety limit ($\sim\!120$ raw units) is not reached in the random-walk protocol and is therefore not used as the NMAE denominator. Position-space metrics are reported in millimetres throughout.

\paragraph{Pressure-space metrics.}
Let $\mathbf{y}_k$ denote the ground-truth actuation command of the $k$-th test sample and $\hat{\mathbf{y}}_k$ the model's prediction, with $k = 1, \ldots, N$ and per-command dimensions indexed by $j = 1, \ldots, n_p$. Let $V_{\max}$ denote the maximum command value of the dataset ($100$ for Dataset 1, $2500$ for Dataset 2), and $\bar{y}$ the scalar mean of the ground-truth commands over all sample--dimension pairs. The four pressure-space metrics were
\begin{align}
  \mathrm{MAE}  &= \frac{1}{N n_p} \sum_{k, j} \bigl| y_{k,j} - \hat{y}_{k,j} \bigr|, \label{eq:mae} \\
  \mathrm{MAPE} &= \frac{100}{|\mathcal{K}|} \sum_{(k, j) \in \mathcal{K}} \frac{\bigl| y_{k,j} - \hat{y}_{k,j} \bigr|}{\bigl| y_{k,j} \bigr|}, \label{eq:mape} \\
  \mathrm{NMAE} &= \frac{\mathrm{MAE}}{V_{\max}}, \label{eq:nmae} \\
  R^{2}         &= 1 - \frac{\sum_{k, j} \bigl( y_{k,j} - \hat{y}_{k,j} \bigr)^{2}}{\sum_{k, j} \bigl( y_{k,j} - \bar{y} \bigr)^{2}}, \label{eq:r2}
\end{align}
where $\mathcal{K} = \{(k, j) : |y_{k,j}| > 0.01 \, V_{\max}\}$ restricts the MAPE sum to samples whose ground-truth command exceeds $1\%$ of the command-range maximum, avoiding divide-by-near-zero inflation of the statistic. MAPE is reported as a percentage and NMAE as a dimensionless fraction of the command range.

\paragraph{Position-space metrics.}
For every closed-loop evaluation, the per-sample Euclidean distance between the target tip position and the forward-evaluated achieved tip position was recorded in millimetres. The resulting distribution was summarised by its mean, its maximum, its $95$th percentile, and its root-mean-square. These statistics are reported per method for the per-sample test-set evaluation, and per method and trajectory in the trajectory-tracking evaluation.

The three repetitions of seed~0 in Dataset 1 provide a physical noise floor against which any inverse-kinematics error is to be interpreted: tip positions recorded under nearly identical actuation commands differ between the repeats owing to optical-marker noise, mounting variability, and small deviations of the commanded pressures themselves. The standard deviation of the per-frame tip position across the three seed-0 trials (with frames paired by nearest neighbour in the six-dimensional pressure-command space) is used as an empirical lower bound for the Dataset 1 closed-loop error. Any model error below this bound is to be read as overfitting to noise rather than as a measurement of inverse-kinematics accuracy.

\paragraph{Paired comparison protocol.}
Where two methods are compared on the same sample set---for example, the tip-only and tip-plus-mid networks---three paired statistics were reported. The first was the fraction of test frames on which one method produced a lower per-sample error than the other. The second was the mean, together with a bootstrap $95\%$ confidence interval, of the per-frame error difference between the two methods. The third was the two-sided Wilcoxon signed-rank test on the paired per-sample errors, which does not assume a normal distribution of the differences. The bootstrap used $10{,}000$ resamples with replacement of the per-frame error differences.

The trajectory-tracking evaluation additionally reports the sum of squared discrete third differences of the pressure command (Eq.~\ref{eq:traj_jerk}) as a proxy for command-sequence jerk, and the wall-clock time required to predict a single command on the CPU as a runtime metric. Runtime is reported in milliseconds.
