\label{sec:datasets}

The first, Dataset 1, was released with the I-Support soft manipulator \cite{manti2016soft} and extended to a two-module configuration by Bianchi et al.\ \cite{bianchi2023learning}. The second, Dataset 2, was provided by the project supervisor and is not otherwise publicly released. Both supply the actuation--pose pairs required for learning-based inverse kinematics (IK). \subsection{Dataset 1 --- I-Support (20\,Hz)}
\label{sec:ds1}

The I-Support platform is a silicone soft manipulator composed of two identical modules connected in series. Each module is 205\,mm long at rest and 60\,mm in diameter. Three McKibben-type fluidic chambers are distributed uniformly at $120^{\circ}$ around each module axis. The second module is rotated by $60^{\circ}$ relative to the first, so the six chambers together cover the full actuation circle. The robot was mounted vertically with the tip pointing downwards, giving an assembled rest length of approximately 410\,mm. The maximum safe chamber pressure is 120\,kPa (1.2\,bar). The pressure--deformation response is nonlinear, with a dead zone below roughly 50\,kPa \cite{manti2016soft}.

Nine reflective markers were attached in three triads: on the fixed base, on the mid-section between the two modules, and on the end-effector. A random-walk pressure sequence drove the six chambers at a sampling rate of 20\,Hz. Six recordings of 4{,}000 frames were provided, covering four random-number-generator seeds (0, 1, 2, 3); the seed-0 trial was repeated three times to quantify run-to-run repeatability. Each frame holds the three-dimensional positions of all nine markers in millimetres, together with the six chamber pressures in raw units where a value near 100 corresponds to 1\,bar. Marker occlusions were present in roughly 0.3\% of the raw frames. The mid- and tip-marker triads were occluded at the same frames, so the same subset remained usable for every input variant considered in this thesis. The base-relative tip distribution is shown in Fig~\ref{fig:ds1_workspace}.

\subsection{Dataset 2 --- three-valve pneumatic recording}
\label{sec:ds2}

Dataset 2 comprises 18{,}072 frames. Each frame contains three valve commands and a three-dimensional tip position. The valve commands are integer-valued within $[0, 2500]$. No calibration to physical pressure was provided with the release, so the commands were treated as an un-calibrated actuation coordinate. Tip positions were supplied in inches and were converted to millimetres (factor 25.4) before further processing.

After conversion, the tip coordinates spanned approximately $[-47.9, 53.8]$\,mm laterally and $[-27.5, 36.9]$\,mm axially, giving a shallow disc-shaped workspace roughly 100\,mm wide and 65\,mm deep (Fig~\ref{fig:ds2_workspace}). No missing values and no duplicate frames were present. The frames are ordered as recorded. Whether this order carries any temporal structure was examined in the exploratory analysis (Section~\ref{sec:eda}); the original order was preserved at every preprocessing stage regardless. The source robot is driven by three valves, but its number of segments, geometry, and physical parameters are not documented in the release. Dataset 2 was therefore treated as a black-box mapping from actuation commands to tip pose, and no geometric assumptions were made without direct verification.

\subsection{Data splits and preprocessing}
\label{sec:splits}

Dataset 1 was split at the seed level. The seed-1 and seed-2 recordings formed the training set. The seed-3 recording served as the validation set. The three repetitions of seed~0 constituted the test set. This arrangement keeps all repeated runs together; intra-seed repeatability can then be reported on the test metric as a physical noise floor for the IK error (Section~\ref{sec:eda}). Frames from the same seed were never mixed across splits. Dataset 2 was split chronologically in the ratio $70/15/15$, yielding 12{,}650 training, 2{,}710 validation, and 2{,}712 test frames. Random shuffling was not applied at any stage.

Tip and mid coordinates in Dataset 1 were expressed relative to the base triad. For each frame, the base position was taken as the mean of the three base markers and subtracted from the mid and tip means. This removes any static translation offset introduced by re-installing the robot between recordings. The correction is sufficient in the absence of rotational drift between sessions; the assumption was checked in the exploratory analysis (Section~\ref{sec:eda}). Frames in which either the mid or the tip triad contained a missing marker were dropped jointly, leaving 23{,}936 usable frames across the six recordings. Joint dropping ensures that the tip-only and tip-plus-mid configurations of Experiment~2 share an identical sample set.

Inputs and outputs were standardised to zero mean and unit variance. Scaler parameters were fitted on the training split only, with input and output scalers fitted independently. For the sequence models in Experiment~3, standardisation was applied before the sliding-window construction. Sliding windows were constrained to remain within a single seed in Dataset 1 and within a single split in Dataset 2, so no window crossed a recording or split boundary.

\section{Exploratory Data Analysis (Exp 0)}
\label{sec:eda}

Each dataset was examined in a brief exploratory analysis before model training. Three questions motivated the analysis. The first was whether the raw data matched the expected format. The second concerned the preprocessing assumptions stated in Section~\ref{sec:splits}. The third, applied only to Dataset 1, quantified the physical noise floor attributable to the data-collection hardware.

Each recording in Dataset 1 was inspected along three axes. First, the per-frame base position was computed, and its spatial standard deviation along each axis was recorded as a check on base-stationarity within a recording. Second, the base-relative tip clouds of all four seeds were overlaid in three dimensions, as a check for rotational drift between recording sessions. Third, intra-seed repeatability was estimated from the three seed-0 trials. For this estimate, each tip sample in one trial was paired with its nearest neighbour in each of the other two trials, using Euclidean distance in the six-dimensional pressure-command space. The tip-position discrepancy between paired frames was then aggregated as a mean, median, and ninety-fifth percentile. Nearest-neighbour matching was preferred over direct index alignment because the seed-0 command sequences were observed to differ slightly across trials.

Dataset 2 was inspected for basic integrity and for the presence of temporal structure. Missing values and duplicate rows were counted. Histograms of the three valve commands and of the three tip coordinates were reviewed to identify any truncation, clipping, or quantisation artefacts. A step-size statistic was used to test whether consecutive rows carry any temporal relationship. The mean Euclidean tip-position step from each row to the next was computed, and the same statistic was measured on a shuffled copy of the recording; a ratio close to one indicates that consecutive rows are no more similar than random pairs, whereas a ratio well below one indicates trajectory-like correlation. Pearson correlations were also computed between each valve command and each tip coordinate, as a preliminary check that the actuation--pose mapping is non-degenerate before any model was fitted.

The numerical summaries and accompanying figures are presented in Chapter~\ref{chap:results}.

\section{Analytical Baseline: PCC Inverse Kinematics}

\subsection{Single-Segment PCC (Exp 1A, Dataset 2)}
\label{sec:pcc_single}

A single-segment, three-chamber piecewise-constant-curvature (PCC) model was used as an analytical inverse-kinematics baseline on Dataset 2. The PCC formulation assumes that the actuator centreline bends as a circular arc of constant curvature, with three fluidic chambers equally spaced at $120^{\circ}$ around the neutral axis and offset from it by a distance $d$ \cite{webster2010review}. The geometric inverse kinematics were taken from Ma et al.\ \cite{ma2022bp}, Eqs.\ 11--17, with three configuration variables: the azimuth $\varphi$ of the bending plane, the bending angle $\theta$, and the arc length $L$ of the centreline.

\paragraph{Geometry.}
Given a target tip position $(x, y, z)$ in the base-relative frame, with the straight-pose axis along $+z$, the configuration was computed as
\begin{align}
  \varphi &= \operatorname{atan2}(y, x), \label{eq:pcc_phi} \\
  \theta  &= \arccos\!\left(\frac{z^{2} - (x^{2} + y^{2})}{z^{2} + (x^{2} + y^{2})}\right), \label{eq:pcc_theta} \\
  L       &= z \, \theta / \sin\theta. \label{eq:pcc_L}
\end{align}
Targets with $z \le 0$ fall outside the single-segment PCC workspace and were flagged as unreachable. The straight-pose singularity ($x = y = 0$) was handled as a special case with $\theta = 0$, $\varphi$ arbitrary, and $L = z$. The configuration was then mapped to three chamber lengths $(\ell_1, \ell_2, \ell_3)$ by
\begin{equation}
  \ell_i = L - \theta \, d \, \cos(\alpha_i - \varphi), \qquad \alpha_i \in \{\pi/2,\ 7\pi/6,\ 11\pi/6\}, \label{eq:pcc_lengths}
\end{equation}
where $\alpha_i$ denotes the azimuth of chamber $i$.

\paragraph{Length-to-actuation map.}
Ma et al.\ \cite{ma2022bp} relate chamber pressure to chamber length through a hyper-elastic constitutive law whose material constants are robot-specific. The material and the physical meaning of the actuation commands in Dataset 2 are not documented (Section~\ref{sec:ds2}), so a simpler data-driven map was used. Each valve command was modelled as a linear function of its chamber-length change,
\begin{equation}
  V_i = a_i \, (\ell_i - L_0) + b_i, \qquad i = 1, 2, 3, \label{eq:pcc_linear}
\end{equation}
where $L_0$ is the reference segment length. This form keeps the number of free parameters small (six coefficients in total) and remains interpretable: the slopes $a_i$ capture how strongly a chamber elongates per unit command, and the intercepts $b_i$ capture the straight-pose offset of the actuation.

\paragraph{Parameter choices.}
The chamber offset was fixed to $d = 12$\,mm, a representative value for the I-Support platform \cite{manti2016soft}, because the geometry of the Dataset 2 robot is not documented. The reference length was set to $L_0 = 1.05 \cdot \max_k \| \mathbf{p}_k \|$, where $\mathbf{p}_k$ denotes the $k$-th training tip position; the multiplier of $1.05$ places $L_0$ slightly above the recorded workspace, so that most training samples satisfy $\ell_i < L_0$ and the fitted slopes are well conditioned. The coefficients $(a_i, b_i)$ in Eq.~\eqref{eq:pcc_linear} were fitted by ordinary least squares on the training split. Training samples whose targets fell outside the PCC workspace were dropped from the fit.

\paragraph{Inference and evaluation.}
At inference, each test tip position was passed through Eqs.~\eqref{eq:pcc_phi}--\eqref{eq:pcc_linear} to produce three predicted valve commands, or an unreachable flag when the target failed the workspace condition. Two groups of errors were reported. On the reachable subset, pressure-space metrics---mean squared error, mean absolute error, mean absolute percentage error on samples with $|V| > 50$, and normalised mean absolute error relative to the full command range---were computed against the ground-truth valve commands. A forward residual was also computed on the reachable subset by inverting Eq.~\eqref{eq:pcc_linear} to recover chamber lengths from the predicted commands, inverting Eq.~\eqref{eq:pcc_lengths} to recover the configuration $(\theta, \varphi, L)$, and finally applying the closed-form PCC forward kinematics to obtain a reconstructed tip position; the Euclidean distance between the reconstructed and the target tip position was reported as the forward residual. For the full test set, a penalised mean was also reported, in which unreachable samples were assigned an error equal to the workspace diameter estimated from the training tip positions. The penalised mean provides a conservative upper bound that can be compared against downstream methods without over-crediting PCC on its favourable subset.

\subsection{Two-Segment PCC (Exp 1B, Dataset 1)}
\label{sec:pcc_multi}

The I-Support manipulator (Section~\ref{sec:ds1}) consists of two PCC-like modules stacked in series, which the single-segment model of Section~\ref{sec:pcc_single} cannot represent. A two-segment extension was implemented following Sokolov et al.\ \cite{sokolov2024twoseg}, who compose two single-segment PCC transforms with a $60^{\circ}$ inter-segment offset that matches the I-Support geometry exactly. No closed-form inverse exists for the compound model; the inverse was therefore solved numerically as an optimisation problem, following the formulation of Keyvanara et al.\ \cite{keyvanara2023geometric}.

\paragraph{Forward kinematics.}
Each segment $s \in \{1, 2\}$ was parameterised by its bending angle $\theta_s$, bending-plane azimuth $\varphi_s$, and elongation $\Delta L_s$ from a nominal rest length $L_s^{(0)} = 205$\,mm \cite{manti2016soft}. The segment arc length was $L_s = L_s^{(0)} + \Delta L_s$. The local transform from the base of segment $s$ to its tip is a displacement
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{t}_s = \frac{L_s}{\theta_s} \begin{pmatrix} (1 - \cos\theta_s) \cos\varphi_s \\ (1 - \cos\theta_s) \sin\varphi_s \\ \sin\theta_s \end{pmatrix},
  \label{eq:pcc_multi_segment_tip}
\end{equation}
combined with a rotation $\mathbf{R}_s$ by $\theta_s$ about the axis $(-\sin\varphi_s,\, \cos\varphi_s,\, 0)$, which is the normal to the bending plane. The limit $\theta_s \to 0$ corresponds to a straight-pose translation by $L_s$ along $+z$.

The compound tip position was obtained by chaining the two segment transforms with a fixed rotation about the local $z$-axis between them, matching the I-Support inter-segment offset:
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{p}_{\mathrm{tip}} = \mathbf{t}_1 + \mathbf{R}_1 \, \mathbf{R}_z(\pi/3) \, \mathbf{t}_2, \label{eq:pcc_multi_fk}
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{R}_z(\pi/3)$ denotes the rotation matrix about $z$ by $60^{\circ}$.

\paragraph{Frame convention.}
Dataset 1's base-relative frame places the robot's rest pose along $-z$ (the arm hangs downward; Section~\ref{sec:ds1}), whereas Eq.~\eqref{eq:pcc_multi_segment_tip} assumes a rest pose along $+z$. Target tip positions were therefore reflected across the $z$-axis before being passed to the inverse kinematics, and reconstructed tips were reflected back before error evaluation.

\paragraph{Inverse kinematics.}
The configuration $\boldsymbol{\xi} = (\theta_1, \varphi_1, \Delta L_1, \theta_2, \varphi_2, \Delta L_2)^{\top}$ is not uniquely determined by the three-dimensional tip, so the inverse was formulated as a box-constrained least-squares optimisation \cite{keyvanara2023geometric},
\begin{equation}
  \min_{\boldsymbol{\xi} \in \mathcal{B}} \; \left\| \mathbf{p}_{\mathrm{target}} - \mathbf{p}_{\mathrm{tip}}(\boldsymbol{\xi}) \right\|^{2}_{2}, \label{eq:pcc_multi_ik}
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{B}$ is the product of per-parameter bounds: $\theta_s \in [0, 2.5]$\,rad, $\varphi_s \in [-\pi, \pi]$, and $\Delta L_s \in [-50, 50]$\,mm per segment. The bending bound exceeds the single-chamber maximum of approximately 2\,rad reported for the I-Support platform \cite{manti2016soft}; the elongation bound was kept conservative to restrict the feasible configuration space to the expected operating envelope. Eq.~\eqref{eq:pcc_multi_ik} was solved by the SLSQP algorithm, with a slight-bend initialisation $(\theta_1, \theta_2) = (0.1,\, 0.1)$\,rad and all other parameters zero, chosen to avoid the $\theta = 0$ singularity of Eq.~\eqref{eq:pcc_multi_segment_tip}.

\paragraph{Warm-starting along a recording.}
The solver returns a single local optimum, and the multi-valued nature of the inverse means that different initialisations can converge to different solution branches. Within each recording, the solver therefore initialised frame $k$ from the solution of frame $k-1$, following the trajectory-continuity protocol of Keyvanara et al.\ \cite{keyvanara2023geometric}. Warm-starting was reset at recording boundaries.

\paragraph{Chamber lengths and pressure calibration.}
For each segment, the configuration $(\theta_s, \varphi_s, L_s)$ was mapped to three chamber lengths by Eq.~\eqref{eq:pcc_lengths}, giving six chamber lengths $(\ell_1, \ldots, \ell_6)$ per frame. A per-chamber linear fit, analogous to Eq.~\eqref{eq:pcc_linear} but with six chambers,
\begin{equation}
  V_i = a_i \, (\ell_i - L_0) + b_i, \qquad i = 1, \ldots, 6, \label{eq:pcc_multi_linear}
\end{equation}
mapped chamber lengths to the recorded pressure commands. The reference length was set to $L_0 = 205$\,mm for every chamber. The coefficients $(a_i, b_i)$ were fitted on the training split by ordinary least squares, using chamber lengths recovered from the training targets through Eqs.~\eqref{eq:pcc_multi_ik}--\eqref{eq:pcc_lengths}.

\paragraph{Evaluation.}
Each test target was passed through Eqs.~\eqref{eq:pcc_multi_ik}--\eqref{eq:pcc_multi_linear} to produce a predicted six-chamber pressure vector. Two families of metrics were reported. First, pressure-space errors---mean absolute error, mean absolute percentage error, and the coefficient of determination $R^{2}$---were computed against the ground-truth commands. Second, a closed-loop position error was obtained by passing the predicted pressures back through the forward map (Eq.~\eqref{eq:pcc_multi_fk}) and measuring the Euclidean distance between the reconstructed and target tips. The geometric IK residual, defined as the SLSQP objective at convergence before the pressure calibration, was also recorded, so that the geometric-fit quality can be separated from the calibration quality.

\section{Feed-Forward Neural IK}
\label{sec:mlp}

The analytical PCC baselines of Sections~\ref{sec:pcc_single}--\ref{sec:pcc_multi} rely on a specific mechanical model of the robot and lose accuracy when its assumptions fail. A feed-forward neural network removes that reliance: the actuation--pose map is learned directly from data, at the cost of modelling capacity that must be regularised by the training set. The approach follows Giorelli et al.\ \cite{giorelli2015nn}, who compared a neural inverse-kinematics model against an analytical Jacobian method on a cable-driven soft arm. Two variants were implemented: Experiment 2A (Section~\ref{sec:mlp_tip}) used the tip position alone as input, representing the minimal-sensing case, while Experiment 2B (Section~\ref{sec:mlp_tipmid}) augmented the input with the mid-section marker triad to test whether shape information improves the inverse on the two-segment Dataset 1 robot.

\subsection{MLP architecture and training (Exp 2A, tip-only)}
\label{sec:mlp_tip}

The network was a fully connected multilayer perceptron. The input was the base-relative tip position $(x, y, z)$ in millimetres (three features), and the output was the actuation command vector: three valve commands for Dataset 2 and six chamber pressures for Dataset 1. Four hidden layers were used, each followed by a ReLU activation; no dropout or weight decay was applied. The hidden widths were set to $[128, 256, 256, 128]$ for Dataset 1 and $[64, 128, 128, 64]$ for Dataset 2. The larger network was allocated to Dataset 1 because its output space is higher-dimensional (six chambers rather than three) and its training split is larger. Fig~\ref{fig:mlp_arch} shows the topology schematically, and Table~\ref{tab:mlp_config} lists the per-variant dimensions and the resulting trainable-parameter counts.

Training used the mean squared error (MSE) loss on the z-score--standardised outputs, the standardisation having been fitted on the training split only (Section~\ref{sec:splits}). Parameter updates used the Adam optimiser with learning rate $10^{-3}$, batch size $64$, and no weight decay. Training was capped at $500$ epochs with early stopping on the validation loss, triggered after $30$ consecutive epochs without improvement. The checkpoint with the lowest validation loss was retained for all reported test metrics.

Test-set predictions were de-standardised through the stored output scaler before any error metric was computed. Four metrics were reported: mean absolute error (MAE) in the original actuation units; mean absolute percentage error (MAPE) computed on samples whose ground-truth pressure exceeded $1\%$ of the command-range maximum, to avoid divide-by-near-zero; normalised MAE (NMAE) expressed as a percentage of the command-range maximum; and the coefficient of determination $R^{2}$. Inference latency was measured as the wall-clock time to predict a single sample on the CPU.

\subsection{Tip-plus-mid ablation (Exp 2B, Dataset 1)}
\label{sec:mlp_tipmid}

The I-Support manipulator is underdetermined from its tip alone: the six-dimensional actuation space maps into a three-dimensional tip space, so multiple configurations can place the end-effector at the same point. A network that receives only the tip must collapse this ambiguity, which can succeed only when the training distribution steers consistently toward one solution branch. If the mid-section of the arm carries shape information that is independent of the tip, adding mid-marker coordinates to the input should reduce the ambiguity and lower the pressure-prediction error.

Experiment 2B tested this hypothesis by extending the tip-only input with the base-relative mean of the mid-section marker triad, producing a six-feature input $(x_{\mathrm{tip}}, y_{\mathrm{tip}}, z_{\mathrm{tip}}, x_{\mathrm{mid}}, y_{\mathrm{mid}}, z_{\mathrm{mid}})$. Every other design choice was kept identical to Experiment 2A on Dataset 1: the same four-layer architecture with widths $[128, 256, 256, 128]$, the same ReLU activations, the same Adam optimiser and learning rate, the same batch size, the same early-stopping policy, and the same random seed. Only the first-layer fan-in changed, from three to six inputs.

Because the Dataset 1 loader drops frames where either the mid or the tip triad is occluded (Section~\ref{sec:splits}), the two variants were trained and tested on an identical set of joint-valid frames. Any per-sample difference in pressure error can therefore be attributed to the input-feature change rather than to a mismatch in the sample set.

The two variants were compared through three paired statistics computed on the common test set: the fraction of test frames on which 2B's per-sample pressure RMSE was lower than 2A's; the mean and bootstrap $95\%$ confidence interval of the per-sample error difference $(\mathrm{err}_{2A} - \mathrm{err}_{2B})$; and the two-sided Wilcoxon signed-rank test on the paired per-sample errors. Aggregate MAE, MAPE, and $R^{2}$ were also reported for each variant.
