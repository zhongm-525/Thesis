\section{Pneumatic soft manipulators}
\label{sec:bg_pneumatic}

The two datasets that underpin this thesis were both recorded on pneumatic soft manipulators. This section describes the design principles of that class of robot (Section~\ref{sec:bg_pneumatic_design}), the I-Support platform from which Dataset 1 was recorded (Section~\ref{sec:bg_pneumatic_isupport}), and the smaller family of three-chamber and three-valve platforms that is relevant to Dataset 2 and to the Ma et al.\ \cite{ma2022bp} benchmark (Section~\ref{sec:bg_pneumatic_others}).

\subsection{Design and actuation principles}
\label{sec:bg_pneumatic_design}

A pneumatic soft manipulator is a continuum robot whose structure deforms under fluid pressure rather than by rotating rigid links at discrete joints. The canonical design is a silicone cylinder with several sealed chambers distributed around its neutral axis; pressurising a subset of the chambers lengthens them asymmetrically relative to the others and bends the cylinder away from the active chambers. When all chambers are pressurised equally the cylinder elongates uniformly, and when only one is pressurised the cylinder bends in the plane defined by that chamber and the axis. The most common arrangement uses three chambers placed at $120^{\circ}$ around the axis, which allows bending in any direction in the plane perpendicular to the axis using only three independent pressures. Commercial and research platforms add fibre reinforcement around the chambers---typically a braided polyester or polyethylene terephthalate sheath---to constrain radial expansion and to channel pressure into axial bending or elongation.

The appeal of pneumatic actuation for soft manipulators is the combination of intrinsic mechanical compliance, safety for unstructured contact with humans, and simplicity at the drive end: pressurised air is a cheap and widely available power source, valves are small, and the actuator itself contains no internal friction surfaces. The cost is that the relationship between the applied pressure and the resulting deformation is strongly nonlinear and history-dependent. The silicone matrix exhibits viscoelastic creep on the order of seconds, so the deformation reached under a given pressure depends on the history of that pressure and not only on its current value. Manufacturing variability is also substantial: lateral displacements of $25$--$30$\,mm have been reported for otherwise identical I-Support chambers as a result of manual assembly tolerances \cite{manti2016soft}. These properties together mean that rigid-robot inverse-kinematics methods do not transfer directly to pneumatic soft arms, and they are the main reason the soft-robotics community has pursued learning-based control strategies \cite{kim2021mlsoft}.

\subsection{The I-Support platform}
\label{sec:bg_pneumatic_isupport}

The I-Support manipulator was developed by Manti et al.\ \cite{manti2016soft} at the BioRobotics Institute of Scuola Superiore Sant'Anna, originally as an assistive robot for the showering of elderly users. A single I-Support module is a silicone cylinder of $205$\,mm rest length and $60$\,mm diameter, with three McKibben-type fluidic chambers arranged at $120^{\circ}$ around the central axis and offset approximately $12$\,mm from it. The module is reinforced with a braided polyethylene terephthalate sheath that is thermally set to a bellows shape, which concentrates pressure-induced deformation into axial bending and elongation rather than radial ballooning. Each chamber can be safely pressurised up to $1.2$\,bar, at which a single-chamber actuation bends the module tip by approximately $118^{\circ}$ from its rest pose and a symmetric three-chamber actuation elongates the module by approximately $85\%$ of its rest length. These two behavioural anchors are the reference values used in the PyElastica calibration of Section~\ref{sec:sim_setup}.

The platform has since been redesigned as an additively-manufactured variant (AM-I-Support) that replaces the original latex--silicone construction with a 3D-printed thermoplastic polyurethane of Shore 80A hardness arranged in three actuator groups at $120^{\circ}$ per module \cite{centurelli2022closed, alessi2024pushing}. Two modules are assembled in series with a $60^{\circ}$ inter-module offset so that the chambers together cover the full actuation circle; chambers $1$--$3$ belong to the proximal module and chambers $4$--$6$ to the distal one. The $60^{\circ}$ offset places chamber~$1$ and chamber~$5$ on the same lateral axis, so simultaneous inflation of the pair is mutually confounding, a point that the data collectors flag explicitly \cite{bianchi2024softoss}. Dataset 1 of this thesis was recorded on this AM variant rather than on the 2016 latex prototype, and the physical-parameter values reported in Chapter~\ref{chap:methods} reflect that provenance.

Dataset 1 of this thesis was recorded on the two-module extension of the I-Support platform introduced by Bianchi et al.\ \cite{bianchi2023learning}. The two modules are stacked in series, rotated by $60^{\circ}$ relative to each other so that the six chambers together cover the full actuation circle, and mounted vertically with the end-effector pointing downwards so that gravity acts along the straight-pose axis rather than perpendicular to it. The assembled arm has a rest length of approximately $410$\,mm. These design choices together make the two-module I-Support a convenient reference platform for data-driven inverse-kinematics work on pneumatic soft robots: the actuation is pressure-only with no tendons or cables, the two-module geometry provides the six-chamber over-parameterisation that motivates multi-valued inverse methods (Section~\ref{sec:disc_interpretation}), and marker-based optical tracking of the base, mid-section, and tip provides a spatial sample of the internal configuration along the arm.

\subsection{Other pneumatic platforms relevant to this work}
\label{sec:bg_pneumatic_others}

Two further pneumatic platforms are relevant to this thesis. The first is the three-chamber single-segment arm of Ma et al.\ \cite{ma2022bp}, a purpose-built test rig developed in 2022 to evaluate a back-propagation neural network for inverse-kinematics prediction. Ma et al.'s arm uses three silicone chambers at $120^{\circ}$ around the axis without a second stage, with documented actuation units in bar and a documented straight-pose origin. Its single-segment three-chamber topology matches the geometric assumptions of the piecewise-constant-curvature model that Section~\ref{sec:bg_ik_analytical} reviews below, and the sub-$5\%$ pressure-space MAPE that Ma et al.\ reported on a $216$-sample dataset is a natural benchmark for data-driven pneumatic inverse kinematics; it is used as a scale marker for the Dataset 2 comparisons of Chapter~\ref{chap:results}.

The second is the undocumented three-valve soft arm from which Dataset 2 was recorded. The dataset was supplied by the project supervisor and is not otherwise in the public literature. From the dataset alone it is known that actuation is driven by three valves on an integer-valued command range $[0, 2500]$, that tip positions were recorded in inches and converted to millimetres, and that the observed workspace is a shallow disc of approximately $100$\,mm lateral extent and $65$\,mm axial extent (Section~\ref{sec:ds2}). The absence of documented geometry, material, and actuation calibration places Dataset 2 at the opposite extreme from Ma et al.'s platform along the documentation axis, and several of the experimental limitations discussed in Chapter~\ref{chap:discussion} trace back directly to this asymmetry.

Beyond these two specific platforms, the last decade has produced a wide variety of pneumatic soft-manipulator designs, including single-chamber bending actuators, modular multi-chamber arms, inflatable robotic trunks, and fibre-reinforced continuum manipulators; Kim et al.\ \cite{kim2021mlsoft} and Alessi et al.\ \cite{alessi2024rodreview} provide recent surveys. The present thesis draws on only two datasets, chosen because they expose complementary design properties---a two-module over-actuated pneumatic recording in Dataset 1 and an under-documented three-valve recording in Dataset 2---that together cover the actuation-redundancy regimes that the method-selection discussion of Chapter~\ref{chap:discussion} depends on.

\section{Kinematic modelling of soft robots}
\label{sec:bg_kinematics}

The inverse-kinematics methods compared in this thesis rely on an underlying model of how actuation maps to arm shape and tip position. This section reviews the three classes of kinematic model relevant to continuum soft robots: the piecewise-constant-curvature (PCC) simplification that dominates the analytical literature (Section~\ref{sec:bg_kin_pcc}), its multi-segment composition for arms with more than one module (Section~\ref{sec:bg_kin_multi}), and the more general Cosserat-rod and finite-element formulations that trade analytical tractability for physical fidelity (Section~\ref{sec:bg_kin_cosserat}).

\subsection{Piecewise constant curvature}
\label{sec:bg_kin_pcc}

The piecewise-constant-curvature (PCC) model approximates a soft manipulator's backbone as a sequence of circular arcs, each parameterised by an arc length $L$, a bending angle $\theta$ (equivalently a curvature $\kappa = \theta / L$), and an azimuth $\varphi$ that selects the plane of the bend. A single segment therefore has three configuration-space degrees of freedom, and the forward map from configuration to tip position takes a standard closed form that Webster and Jones \cite{webster2010review} derived in the most widely cited survey of continuum-robot kinematics. The appeal of PCC is its tractability: the forward kinematics is a closed-form expression in $(L, \theta, \varphi)$, the Jacobian can be written analytically, and for a single segment the inverse kinematics reduces to a system of three equations in three unknowns that can be solved in microseconds. These properties have made PCC the standard analytical baseline for pneumatic and cable-driven continuum manipulators through most of the last decade.

The tractability comes at the cost of a restrictive modelling assumption. PCC requires that the curvature is constant along every point of a segment, which in turn requires that the forces and moments acting along the segment are uniform. This assumption is approximately met on short, stiff, tendon-driven arms in which gravity and external contact are small compared with the actuation-induced bending moment. It breaks down on longer pneumatic arms subject to gravity, on arms with distributed mass or stiffness variation along the backbone, and on arms where the fluid-induced bending moment itself is not uniform. Published reviews routinely identify PCC's constant-curvature assumption as the principal source of residual error when the predicted and measured tip positions are compared on real hardware \cite{webster2010review, alessi2024rodreview}. The Dataset 2 results of Chapter~\ref{chap:results} provide one further empirical instance: the single-segment PCC baseline reaches a $22$\,mm forward residual on a workspace of only $100$\,mm because an elongation coupling that a constant-$L$ PCC cannot represent dominates the error (Section~\ref{sec:res_pcc_single}).

\subsection{Multi-segment extensions}
\label{sec:bg_kin_multi}

Multi-segment soft manipulators such as the two-module I-Support of Section~\ref{sec:bg_pneumatic_isupport} are modelled by chaining single-segment PCC transforms in series. Sokolov et al.\ \cite{sokolov2024twoseg} present an explicit two-segment derivation with a $60^{\circ}$ inter-segment rotation that corresponds to the I-Support geometry exactly, and this is the formulation adopted for the two-segment PCC baseline of Section~\ref{sec:pcc_multi}. The forward map remains closed-form under composition, but the inverse map loses its single-valued character: a two-segment arm typically has six or more actuation degrees of freedom that map into a three-dimensional tip position, and the resulting inverse is a relation rather than a function.

Two main strategies have emerged for solving the multi-segment inverse. The first is an optimisation-based search over the configuration space, constrained to physically plausible per-segment bounds; Keyvanara et al.\ \cite{keyvanara2023geometric} use sequential least-squares quadratic programming for this purpose, with a trajectory-continuity warm-start from the previous target that this thesis adopts in Section~\ref{sec:pcc_multi}. The second is a Jacobian-based iteration on the analytically tractable forward map, as developed for cable-driven continuum arms by Giorelli et al.\ \cite{giorelli2015nn}. Both approaches recover the configuration of the arm to within numerical tolerance, but they leave open how that configuration maps to the measurable actuation command; that step is straightforward when the actuation units are documented and calibrated, and it collapses under ordinary least squares when many distinct configurations share nearly the same chamber-length vector. The latter failure mode is the one observed on Dataset 1 in Section~\ref{sec:res_pcc_multi}.

\subsection{Beyond PCC: Cosserat rod and finite-element approaches}
\label{sec:bg_kin_cosserat}

When the constant-curvature assumption of PCC is violated, Cosserat-rod theory provides a more general framework that models the backbone as a continuous elastic body with cross-section orientation and position evolving along an arc-length parameter. The Cosserat rod captures nonconstant curvature under distributed loads, couples bending with elongation and torsion through the material's elastic moduli, and produces configurations that PCC cannot reach by construction. Alessi et al.\ \cite{alessi2024rodreview} review the use of rod models in continuum and soft-robot control and distinguish four main rod theories that differ in the deformation modes they represent and in how they are discretised for numerical integration. The PyElastica library of Gazzola et al.\ \cite{gazzola2018pyelastica}, which this thesis uses for the simulator of Section~\ref{sec:sim_setup}, implements the special-Cosserat-rod formulation with a position-Verlet integrator that scales linearly in the number of discretisation elements.

Higher-fidelity modelling is possible with fully three-dimensional finite-element approaches, in which the continuum is discretised into volumetric elements rather than a one-dimensional rod. Finite-element methods offer the richest physical accuracy---including contact, material nonlinearity, and stress distribution in cross-section---at substantially higher computational cost and with far more complex parameter calibration than either PCC or Cosserat-rod models. Both Cosserat-rod and finite-element models share the property that their inverse is expensive to compute directly, which is why learning-based methods that fit a fast neural surrogate to expensive simulator trajectories---the sim-to-real approach of Fang et al.\ \cite{fang2022jacobian} and of the PyElastica pipeline evaluated in Chapter~\ref{chap:results}---have become a standard route around the modelling-versus-tractability trade-off.

The practical outcome of the trade-off is that PCC remains the standard analytical baseline in soft-robot inverse-kinematics work despite its limitations: it is fast, interpretable, and accurate enough for short single-segment arms where its assumptions approximately hold. Cosserat-rod models enter when gravity, elongation, or nonconstant loading cannot be neglected, and finite-element methods are reserved for design-space studies in which simulation time is not a control-loop constraint. This thesis uses PCC as a classical reference in Section~\ref{sec:pcc_single}, the Sokolov two-segment composition in Section~\ref{sec:pcc_multi}, and a PyElastica Cosserat rod as the basis of the sim-to-real study in Section~\ref{sec:sim_setup}, covering the three regimes of the trade-off in a single experimental programme.

\section{Inverse kinematics methods}
\label{sec:bg_ik}

This section reviews the four families of inverse-kinematics method compared in this thesis. Analytical methods invert the kinematic model of Section~\ref{sec:bg_kinematics} directly (Section~\ref{sec:bg_ik_analytical}). Feed-forward learning methods train a regressor from target pose to actuation command (Section~\ref{sec:bg_ik_mlp}). Recurrent learning methods add a temporal window to capture history-dependent effects (Section~\ref{sec:bg_ik_lstm}). Jacobian-learned methods learn the forward map and invert it iteratively at run time (Section~\ref{sec:bg_ik_jacobian}).

\subsection{Analytical inverse kinematics}
\label{sec:bg_ik_analytical}

The analytical route derives the inverse from the closed-form kinematic model of the arm. For a single-segment PCC arm the inverse is itself closed form: given a target tip position, the three configuration parameters $(L, \theta, \varphi)$ can be recovered by direct trigonometric inversion of the arc formula, and the three chamber lengths then follow from the chamber arrangement around the axis \cite{webster2010review}. The extension to multi-section arms is not closed form under composition, but Jones and Walker \cite{jones2006multisection} showed that the forward chain can be inverted iteratively by a Jacobian method that applies standard robot-kinematics techniques to the PCC configuration vector, and this formulation remains the reference analytical baseline for segmented continuum arms. Rucker and Webster \cite{rucker2011statics} subsequently extended the analytical route to a Cosserat-rod formulation with general tendon routing and external loading, which covers the configurations that PCC cannot reach; the cost is a considerably more expensive forward evaluation that does not admit a closed-form inverse.

Giorelli et al.\ \cite{giorelli2015nn} provide the most rigorous empirical comparison of analytical inverse kinematics with its learning-based alternatives on a real soft-arm platform. On a cable-driven soft arm with known parameters, their analytical Jacobian method reached a mean tip error of $15.12$\,mm; the same method degraded rapidly when sub-millimetre manufacturing defects in the cable routing were introduced, with the error roughly tripling at a $0.25$\,mm defect. The implication for the present thesis is that an analytical inverse is competitive only when the physical model is both faithful and accurately calibrated; on the undocumented pneumatic arm of Dataset 2, and on the two-module I-Support arm of Dataset 1 with manufacturing asymmetries of the order of $25$\,mm (Section~\ref{sec:bg_pneumatic_design}), analytical methods are used here as classical reference baselines (Sections~\ref{sec:pcc_single}, \ref{sec:pcc_multi}) rather than as competitive methods.

\subsection{Feed-forward neural inverse kinematics}
\label{sec:bg_ik_mlp}

Feed-forward neural networks trained to map a target tip position to the corresponding actuation command have become the simplest data-driven alternative to analytical inverse kinematics. Giorelli et al.\ \cite{giorelli2015nn} found that a small fully-connected network with only six hidden units outperformed the analytical Jacobian method on the cable-driven arm discussed above by about a factor of two on mean tip error, and by roughly $2{,}000\times$ on inference time. Ma et al.\ \cite{ma2022bp} reproduced this pattern on a purpose-built three-chamber pneumatic arm and reported a pressure-space mean absolute percentage error below $5\%$ on a $216$-sample dataset, which is used as the scale marker for the Dataset 2 benchmarks of this thesis. Subsequent work has expanded the architecture space to deeper networks on larger datasets, and Kim et al.\ \cite{kim2021mlsoft} review this line of work in their survey of machine-learning approaches to soft-robot control.

The appeal of the feed-forward route is its robustness to model mismatch: the network learns whatever input--output mapping the data exhibits, without requiring a calibrated physical model. Its limitation is that the mapping it learns on a multi-valued inverse is the posterior mean over valid solutions rather than any single solution (Section~\ref{sec:disc_interpretation}). On a two-segment arm the mean prediction may not correspond to any physically realisable configuration, and the feed-forward network then minimises its squared pressure error at the cost of closed-loop position error. This thesis uses the feed-forward network as the primary data-driven inverse (Section~\ref{sec:mlp_tip}) and ablates the effect of adding partial-shape sensing to its input (Section~\ref{sec:mlp_tipmid}).

\subsection{Recurrent and temporal inverse kinematics}
\label{sec:bg_ik_lstm}

Pneumatic soft actuators exhibit hysteresis, viscoelastic creep, and pneumatic flow delay (Section~\ref{sec:bg_pneumatic_design}), all of which make the pressure-to-pose mapping depend on the recent history of the pressure as well as on its current value. Recurrent neural networks---particularly the long short-term memory (LSTM) architecture introduced by Hochreiter and Schmidhuber \cite{hochreiter1997lstm}---are a standard architectural choice for regression tasks in which history is informative, and they have been adapted to soft-robot control in a growing body of work. Thuruthel et al.\ \cite{thuruthel2019modelbased} used a recurrent forward model as the backbone of a model-based reinforcement-learning controller for a soft manipulator, demonstrating that a learned dynamic model can close the loop around a continuum arm without a pre-specified physical model.

An alternative to recurrent architectures is the use of parametrised hysteresis models such as the Preisach operator and the Bouc-Wen differential equation, which express the history dependence explicitly and can be fitted to measured data. The literature offers mixed results on whether recurrent networks or parametrised hysteresis models give the better inverse on real soft robots; the choice appears to depend on how informative the observable features are about the internal state of the arm. The Dataset 1 results of this thesis (Section~\ref{sec:res_lstm}) and the interpretation of Section~\ref{sec:disc_temporal} provide one data point in this comparison: on a pneumatic two-segment arm, a recurrent network improves over a feed-forward network only when the input features already identify the internal configuration, suggesting that the history-dependent signal is carried by the internal state rather than by the tip trajectory alone.

\subsection{Jacobian-learned inverse kinematics}
\label{sec:bg_ik_jacobian}

A hybrid route combines a learned forward model with an iterative inverse solver. Fang et al.\ \cite{fang2022jacobian} proposed the Jacobian-learned inverse kinematics scheme that this thesis adopts. A feed-forward network is first trained to predict tip position from actuation command; at inference time, the Jacobian of this forward network is computed by reverse-mode automatic differentiation and used in a damped-least-squares update that iteratively refines the actuation command until the forward-mapped position reaches the target. Because the forward map is single-valued, it is easier to learn than the multi-valued inverse; because the iterative solver commits to a single solution near its initialisation, it does not suffer from the posterior-mean averaging that compromises a direct-regression network on a multi-valued inverse.

The Fang approach is a descendant of the analytical Jacobian iteration of Giorelli et al.\ \cite{giorelli2015nn}, with the analytical model replaced by a learned one. The replacement buys robustness to model mismatch---a network can fit a real soft robot with unmodelled hysteresis and manufacturing asymmetry where an analytical model cannot---at the cost of the forward network's test-set accuracy becoming a ceiling on the iterative inverse (Section~\ref{sec:disc_jacobian_vs_mlp}). Fang et al.'s other contribution, a sim-to-real transfer layer that adapts a simulator-trained network to a small real-data budget, was evaluated in this thesis under the workspace-geometry conditions of Dataset 2 (Section~\ref{sec:res_sim_transfer}) and is revisited in Section~\ref{sec:disc_sim}.

\section{Simulation and sim-to-real transfer}
\label{sec:bg_sim}

Soft-robot simulation enables data generation, controller training, and sim-to-real transfer studies that would be impractical to run directly on hardware. This section reviews the simulator used in this thesis (Section~\ref{sec:bg_sim_pyelastica}) and the main strategies that the literature has developed for transferring a sim-trained controller to a real platform (Section~\ref{sec:bg_sim_s2r}).

\subsection{PyElastica for soft-robot simulation}
\label{sec:bg_sim_pyelastica}

The simulator used in this thesis is PyElastica, introduced by Gazzola et al.\ \cite{gazzola2018pyelastica} as a Python implementation of the special Cosserat-rod formulation discussed in Section~\ref{sec:bg_kin_cosserat}. A PyElastica rod is discretised into a sequence of nodes and elements along its centreline, and its state evolves under the combined action of gravity, internal elastic forces, damping, and user-specified actuation moments and forces. Integration uses a position-Verlet stepper, which is symplectic and therefore preserves long-horizon energy behaviour in a way that an explicit Euler integrator would not. The library is open-source and Python-native, which simplifies its integration with the PyTorch training pipelines that most recent soft-robot inverse-kinematics work uses.

Alternatives to PyElastica occupy different points on the fidelity--tractability trade-off reviewed in Section~\ref{sec:bg_kin_cosserat}. The SOFA framework provides a full three-dimensional finite-element solver with support for rich contact and material models, but at substantially higher per-simulation cost and with a more involved parameter-calibration workflow. Differentiable-physics frameworks trade physical completeness for end-to-end differentiability, which enables gradient-based design and controller optimisation at the cost of a more restricted material law. Commercial multi-body simulators rarely support the large deformations that soft manipulators exhibit without bespoke extensions. For the sim-to-real purposes of this thesis---generating a pre-training dataset for a network that will subsequently be fine-tuned on real data---PyElastica's combination of Cosserat-level fidelity, open-source availability, and modest per-simulation cost made it a pragmatic choice.

\subsection{Sim-to-real strategies}
\label{sec:bg_sim_s2r}

Transferring a learned controller from a simulator to a real robot is a recurring challenge across robotics, and soft robots inherit it in a particularly difficult form because their constitutive laws and manufacturing tolerances are harder to model than those of rigid systems (Section~\ref{sec:bg_pneumatic_design}). The literature offers four main strategies, each addressing a different slice of the reality gap.

The first strategy is \emph{direct transfer}: a network is trained on simulator data and deployed on the real robot with no real-data adaptation. This is typically a baseline rather than a production choice, because the real robot and the simulator almost always disagree on some aspect of the dynamics and a sim-trained network cannot be expected to generalise to the real distribution. The Strategy-C configuration of Section~\ref{sec:res_sim_transfer} is an instance of direct transfer; its $R^{2} = -1.71$ on Dataset 2 is a characteristic failure mode.

The second strategy is \emph{fine-tuning} a sim-pre-trained network on a small real-data budget. This is the most common soft-robotics route and is the one adopted by Fang et al.\ \cite{fang2022jacobian}, who reported a positive transfer result on a pneumatic platform for which a digital twin could be constructed. The cost is that the fine-tuning step discards part of what the pre-training learned whenever the simulator distribution does not overlap the real one; the Strategy-B configuration of Section~\ref{sec:res_sim_transfer} is an instance of this strategy.

The third strategy is \emph{domain randomisation}, in which the simulator's parameters---mass, friction, actuator gains, and so on---are sampled from distributions that span the expected real-world range during training, so that the learned network is robust to any single parameter choice. Peng et al.\ \cite{peng2018simtoreal} demonstrated dynamics randomisation as a sim-to-real route for rigid-robot control and showed that policies trained with randomised simulators can transfer to the real world without any real-data fine-tuning. Domain randomisation is attractive when the reality gap can be captured by a small number of easily varied parameters; for soft robots, whose gaps include material hysteresis, viscoelastic creep, and manufacturing asymmetries that are difficult to parameterise analytically, the strategy is harder to apply directly.

The fourth strategy is \emph{real-to-sim calibration}, in which real data are used to tune the simulator itself so that its outputs match the real robot on a held-out calibration sequence. This reverses the direction of transfer---real data teaches the simulator rather than the simulator teaching the controller---and it typically works best when the real platform is sufficiently documented that the simulator's structure is already close to correct and only its parameters need fitting. The Section~\ref{sec:res_sim_cal} calibration of the PyElastica rod to the Manti behavioural anchors is a simple form of this strategy; the more elaborate version would calibrate against the Dataset 2 robot's own data, which was not available in this project.

Across these four strategies the soft-robotics sim-to-real literature converges on one recurring finding: the transfer value of a simulator is bounded above by its geometric and kinematic overlap with the real robot, and efforts to close the reality gap through architectural or regularisation tricks do not overcome a simulator whose workspace does not overlap the real one. This is the finding that the PyElastica experiments of Chapter~\ref{chap:results} reproduce on Dataset 2 and that the post-hoc analysis of Section~\ref{sec:disc_sim} dissects.

\section{Evaluation metrics and benchmarks}
\label{sec:bg_metrics}

This section surveys the metrics used to evaluate soft-robot inverse-kinematics methods in the literature (Sections~\ref{sec:bg_metrics_pose}, \ref{sec:bg_metrics_traj}) and identifies the published headline results that serve as scale markers for this thesis (Section~\ref{sec:bg_metrics_baselines}).

\subsection{Pose error metrics}
\label{sec:bg_metrics_pose}

Two families of error metric are in common use for soft-robot inverse kinematics. The first measures \emph{position-space} error---the Euclidean distance between a target tip position and the position actually achieved once the predicted actuation has been applied. Published work typically reports the mean, the $95$th percentile, and the maximum of this distance over a test set, sometimes together with the root mean square. Because soft-robot platforms span arm lengths from about $100$\,mm to several metres, many authors also report the error normalised by arm length, for example the $7.35$\,mm mean tip error (corresponding to $2.62\%$ of a $280$\,mm arm) reported by Giorelli et al.\ \cite{giorelli2015nn}. Normalised reporting is standard practice when comparing across platforms and is the form used in the cross-platform table of Section~\ref{sec:disc_benchmarks}.

The second family measures \emph{actuation-space} (or \emph{pressure-space}) error, the difference between predicted and ground-truth actuation commands on the same test frames. Mean absolute error (MAE) in the native actuation unit is the simplest form and is directly comparable across runs on the same dataset; mean absolute percentage error (MAPE) normalises by the ground-truth magnitude and is the metric reported by Ma et al.\ \cite{ma2022bp}. MAPE is sensitive to near-zero ground-truth commands, and most implementations apply a small-value threshold to avoid divide-by-near-zero inflation; the definition used in this thesis (Eq.~\eqref{eq:mape}) excludes samples with $|y| < 1\%$ of the maximum command. The coefficient of determination $R^{2}$ captures the fraction of ground-truth variance explained by the predictor and is widely used for regression tasks; a negative $R^{2}$ indicates a predictor worse than a constant regressor returning the per-output mean, which is the failure mode observed for the sim-only network of Section~\ref{sec:res_sim_transfer}.

Comparing pressure-space metrics across platforms is more delicate than comparing position-space metrics, because pressure commands have different ranges, different physical units, and different zero-frames on different platforms. Where the actuation units are documented---as they are for Ma et al.'s three-chamber arm but not for Dataset 2 of this thesis---headline MAPE numbers are directly comparable between studies; where the units are undocumented, any cross-study comparison has to be mediated by a position-space arbiter, typically a learned forward-kinematics network (Section~\ref{sec:res_jacobian}).

\subsection{Trajectory tracking metrics}
\label{sec:bg_metrics_traj}

A second class of evaluation considers how well an inverse-kinematics method drives the tip along a target trajectory rather than how well it predicts isolated targets drawn from a test set. Trajectory-tracking metrics extend the position-space metrics above to a sequence of targets and add two further dimensions specific to control-relevant evaluation. The first is smoothness of the predicted actuation command, commonly quantified either as the integral of squared jerk---the third time derivative of the command---or as the sum of squared discrete third differences that approximates it on a sampled sequence. Smoother commands reduce actuator wear, valve chatter, and power consumption. The second additional dimension is the per-step inference time, which determines the maximum control-loop rate at which a given method can be deployed.

The specific trajectory shapes used vary by platform and application, but four recur across soft-robot papers: circles and figures-of-eight for planar bending, squares for testing corner handling, and helical spirals for probing the axial workspace. Chapter~\ref{chap:methods} adopts a four-trajectory protocol with these shapes (Section~\ref{sec:traj}), and Section~\ref{sec:metrics} defines the specific tracking, smoothness, and inference-time metrics used. The trajectory-tracking evaluation of Section~\ref{sec:res_traj} is the setting in which several methods that rank one way on per-sample metrics either converge or reverse their ranking, making it the single most informative comparison protocol in this thesis.

\subsection{Published baselines}
\label{sec:bg_metrics_baselines}

Three published results serve as scale markers for the quantitative findings of this thesis and are introduced in detail in Section~\ref{sec:disc_benchmarks}. Giorelli et al.\ \cite{giorelli2015nn} reported a mean tip error of $7.35$\,mm ($2.62\%$ of arm length) for a feed-forward network and $15.12$\,mm ($5.40\%$) for an analytical Jacobian on a $280$\,mm cable-driven soft arm, establishing the benchmark for feed-forward-versus-analytical comparison on an unmodelled platform. Ma et al.\ \cite{ma2022bp} reported a pressure-space MAPE below $5\%$ for a back-propagation neural network trained on $216$ samples of a purpose-built three-chamber pneumatic arm, establishing the benchmark for learning-based inverse kinematics on pneumatic platforms with documented actuation units. Fang et al.\ \cite{fang2022jacobian} reported sub-millimetre closed-loop position error for a Jacobian-learned iteration on a pneumatic soft robot with a digital-twin simulator, establishing the benchmark for sim-to-real transfer via a learned forward map.

None of the three uses exactly the same robot, dataset, or metric as this thesis, and the numerical comparisons in Section~\ref{sec:disc_benchmarks} are therefore indicative rather than definitive. Collected together they bracket the accuracy range that cleanly characterised soft-robot platforms have attained in the last decade---from the few-percent-of-arm-length accuracy that a small feed-forward network can reach on an undocumented platform, through the sub-$5\%$ pressure MAPE that a purpose-built platform with calibrated units can achieve, to the sub-millimetre closed-loop accuracy that a Jacobian-learned iteration with a digital twin can reach on a pneumatic arm. This thesis's quantitative findings are positioned against this range in Section~\ref{sec:disc_benchmarks}.

\section{Summary and research gap}
\label{sec:bg_gap}

\paragraph{Summary of the literature.}
Sections~\ref{sec:bg_pneumatic}--\ref{sec:bg_metrics} have surveyed the soft-robot inverse-kinematics literature along five axes. On pneumatic manipulator design (Section~\ref{sec:bg_pneumatic}), the field has converged on silicone cylinders with three or six fluidic chambers arranged at $120^{\circ}$, fibre-reinforced to channel pressure into axial bending and elongation, with the I-Support platform as a representative two-module instance. On kinematic modelling (Section~\ref{sec:bg_kinematics}), piecewise-constant-curvature models dominate because of their closed-form tractability, multi-segment extensions are derivable but produce multi-valued inverses, and Cosserat-rod and finite-element formulations are the standard tools when the constant-curvature assumption breaks down. On inverse kinematics (Section~\ref{sec:bg_ik}), four method families compete: analytical inversion, feed-forward regression, recurrent regression, and Jacobian-learned iteration. On simulation (Section~\ref{sec:bg_sim}), PyElastica is the current open-source standard for Cosserat-level fidelity at reasonable cost, and four sim-to-real strategies---direct transfer, fine-tuning, domain randomisation, and real-to-sim calibration---cover the routes the literature has explored. On evaluation (Section~\ref{sec:bg_metrics}), the community reports position-space and pressure-space metrics with various normalisations, and three published results (Giorelli et al.\ \cite{giorelli2015nn}, Ma et al.\ \cite{ma2022bp}, and Fang et al.\ \cite{fang2022jacobian}) bracket the accuracy range currently attainable on cleanly characterised platforms.

\paragraph{Research gap.}
Three observations about the surveyed literature set up the gap that this thesis addresses. First, each of the four inverse-kinematics method families has typically been evaluated in isolation rather than side by side on identical data. Giorelli et al.\ \cite{giorelli2015nn} compared feed-forward and analytical Jacobian methods but did not include recurrent or learned-Jacobian variants; Ma et al.\ \cite{ma2022bp} evaluated only a feed-forward network; Fang et al.\ \cite{fang2022jacobian} evaluated only a Jacobian-learned method. A reader trying to choose between method families for a new platform has to assemble these results across separate papers, datasets, and platforms, with attendant confounds in calibration and evaluation protocol. Second, the role of partial-shape sensing in resolving the multi-valued inverses of multi-segment arms has been proposed in principle but not quantified by an ablation that changes only the input-feature vector of an otherwise identical model on identical test frames. Third, sim-to-real transfer on pneumatic soft arms has been reported on platforms for which a digital twin could be constructed \cite{fang2022jacobian}, but the typical situation of an undocumented real arm whose physical model must be borrowed from a reference platform has not been examined systematically, leaving open the question of what transfers and what does not when the simulator is only architecturally similar to the target.

\paragraph{Contributions of this thesis.}
The contributions of this thesis address each part of the above gap in turn. First, Chapter~\ref{chap:results} reports a head-to-head comparison of analytical piecewise-constant-curvature inverse kinematics, feed-forward neural inverse kinematics, recurrent neural inverse kinematics, and Jacobian-learned inverse kinematics on two pneumatic datasets with complementary actuation redundancy: a two-module six-chamber I-Support recording and an undocumented three-valve recording, under identical evaluation protocols. Second, the feed-forward and recurrent methods are evaluated in both tip-only and tip-plus-mid configurations on the six-chamber recording, providing a paired statistical measurement of the value of partial-shape sensing that is not available from any single prior study. Third, a sim-to-real pipeline based on an I-Support-inspired PyElastica rod is evaluated on the undocumented three-valve platform at four real-data budgets, together with a decomposition of the reality gap into its geometric, constitutive, viscoelastic, and actuation-dynamics components (Section~\ref{sec:disc_sim}). Fourth, the combined empirical results are synthesised in Chapter~\ref{chap:discussion} into a three-property decision criterion---actuation redundancy, sensing coverage, and target-domain overlap---that offers a compact route through the method-selection space that the isolated studies above leave implicit. Each of these contributions is developed in the chapters that follow.
