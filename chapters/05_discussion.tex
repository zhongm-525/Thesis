\chapter{Discussion}
\label{chap:discussion}

\section{Interpretation of results}
\label{sec:disc_interpretation}

No single method dominated every regime: each won on some dataset and lost elsewhere. This section reads the results as one story, argues that the regimes depend on three physical properties, and explains the least obvious findings.

\paragraph{Classical baselines failed for different reasons.}
Single-segment PCC hit a $22$\,mm residual on Dataset~2's $\sim$$100$\,mm workspace because an elongation signal ($r = +0.737$ with total pressurisation) dominates and a constant-$L$ arc cannot represent it. Two-segment PCC reached a geometric residual of $3 \times 10^{-3}$\,mm on Dataset~1 but a $51$\,mm closed-loop error: the multi-valued inverse lands on different solution branches across training frames, and ordinary least-squares calibration collapses to the per-chamber mean. Correct geometry therefore does not guarantee a competitive inverse---both configuration-space and actuation-space well-posedness are required.

\paragraph{Data-driven regression tolerates ill-posedness by averaging.}
A squared-loss regressor maps a multi-valued inverse to the per-tip posterior mean over valid pressures. Dataset~1's tip-only network plateaued at $R^{2} = 0.32$ after four epochs (under-determined); Dataset~2's single-valued inverse reached $R^{2} = 0.93$ with the same architecture. The predictive ceiling is set by the posterior's concentration, not by the network's capacity: the right diagnostic is actuation redundancy, not architecture choice.

\paragraph{Mid-marker lifts the ceiling; temporal context only helps once it does.}
Adding the mid-marker was the single largest improvement: $R^{2}$ rose from $0.32$ to $0.44$ (MLP) and from $0.28$ to $0.51$ (LSTM). The tip-only LSTM was marginally worse than the tip-only MLP, so temporal context is uninformative without observable internal state; combined with the mid marker, the recurrent network extracts the internal-state trajectory, which is physically meaningful only once the state itself is observable.

\paragraph{Position vs pressure trade-off is an inverse-problem property.}
On multi-valued inverses a direct regressor minimises pressure error (posterior mean); Jacobian iteration commits to a single branch and minimises position error. Dataset~2 showed a $33\%$ pressure-MAE increase for a $71\%$ position-error reduction (Table~\ref{tab:res_jacobian_pos}); Dataset~1 similar in sign, smaller in magnitude. The two methods therefore measure different objectives---pick by downstream use.

\paragraph{Sim-to-real dominated by workspace geometry, not sample count.}
Strategy~B matched Strategy~A at every budget; Strategy~C reached $R^{2} = -1.71$. The synthetic cloud was $5\times$ Dataset~2's extent on each axis and differently centred, so the prior pointed at an irrelevant region; fine-tuning on real data simply discarded it. A Manti-inspired rod is not a drop-in prior for an undocumented robot---the $720$-sample count was not the ceiling.

\paragraph{A compact decision criterion.}
The regime is set by three arm properties. (1) \emph{Actuation redundancy}: when DOF exceed tip-DOF, Jacobian iteration reaches the tolerance floor. (2) \emph{Sensing coverage}: when the tip under-identifies the internal state, one partial shape observation beats any network-side intervention. (3) \emph{Target-domain overlap}: a simulator prior is useful only when its synthetic workspace covers the real operating region. These three account for every regime-specific finding in Chapter~\ref{chap:results}.

\paragraph{Ma et al.: a $6.9\times$ MAPE gap attributable to calibration metadata.}
Ma et al.\ reach $<5\%$ MAPE on a purpose-built three-chamber arm with $216$ samples; Dataset~2 reaches $34.4\%$ with $12{,}650$. With sample size ruled out, the gap comes from Dataset~2's undocumented units/zero-frame and the random-walk (vs quasi-static) protocol; the LSTM extension yielded only $34.5 \to 34.2\%$, consistent with weak temporal structure on Dataset~2 rather than the absence of a physical hysteresis effect.

\paragraph{Giorelli et al.: feed-forward vs analytical ordering reproduced.}
Giorelli et al.\ reported a $2.05\times$ FF-over-analytical advantage. This thesis reproduces the ordering on Dataset~2 (FF MAE $150.1$ vs PCC $285.2$, $1.9\times$). The relative position-space error is $7.4\%$ here (Dataset~2 tracking) against Giorelli's $2.62\%$, wider because of the undocumented units/zero-frame and Dataset~2's smaller absolute workspace.

\paragraph{Fang et al.: sub-mm Jacobian-IK replicated on Dataset~1.}
Fang et al.'s sub-mm learned-Jacobian result \cite{fang2022jacobian} is reproduced here at $0.71$\,mm (per-sample) and $0.77$\,mm (trajectory) on Dataset~1, using reference hyperparameters. Fang's sim-to-real extension was not validated: the negative transfer here reflects the Manti-inspired rod's workspace mismatch with Dataset~2, not the method.

The three numbers are scale markers, not targets; the survivable claim is Dataset~1's sub-mm Jacobian closed-loop error on a real two-module pneumatic recording, matching Fang's order of magnitude.

\section{The role of mid-segment information on Dataset 1}
\label{sec:disc_mid}

\paragraph{Tip-only is under-determined.}
Six chamber pressures map into a three-dimensional tip: under PCC, each tip admits a continuous one-parameter family of per-segment configurations. The tip-only feed-forward network plateaus at $R^{2} = 0.32$ after four epochs, with chambers~2 and 4 at chance ($R^{2} \le 0.04$; Fig~\ref{fig:res_mlp_pred_vs_actual})---the remaining variance belongs to the posterior, not to model capacity.

\paragraph{Mid-marker concentrates the posterior.}
The mid-marker centroid adds three observations that, under PCC, identify the segment-1 endpoint and over-determine the per-segment $(\theta, \varphi, \Delta L)$ to optical noise. The ablation (same architecture, training, seed) cuts MAE $20.13 \to 17.05$ ($-15\%$), lifts $R^{2}$ $0.32 \to 0.44$, and gives Wilcoxon $p \approx 2.4 \times 10^{-211}$---an unusually clean information-content intervention.

\paragraph{Temporal context amplifies the mid-marker signal, not the tip.}
Tip-only LSTM is marginally \emph{worse} than tip-only MLP on Dataset~1 ($R^{2}$ $0.32 \to 0.28$); tip-plus-mid LSTM reaches $R^{2} = 0.51$ ($+17\%$ over tip-plus-mid MLP). Historical tip samples cannot pin the internal state; once the mid marker does, the recurrent network extracts the internal-state trajectory, which carries velocity and hysteresis information. Temporal modelling is thus contingent on the features already resolving under-identification.

The mid-marker benefit is contingent on a real mid-marker signal at deployment: the trajectory evaluation substituted the training-distribution centroid and the network extrapolated off its training slice to $19.08$\,mm mean tracking error (the worst Dataset~1 entry in Table~\ref{tab:res_overall_ds1}), so any controller intending to use a tip-plus-mid network would need a running shape estimator of comparable quality.

\section{Temporal modelling and hysteresis}
\label{sec:disc_temporal}

Only tip-plus-mid on Dataset~1 shows a clean gain ($R^{2}$ $0.44 \to 0.51$). Tip-only Dataset~1 is marginally worse; tip-only Dataset~2 is marginally better ($-4.8\%$ MAE). On smooth trajectories all recurrent and feed-forward variants sit within $1$\,mm of each other. The uniform benefit expected from hysteresis-dominated residual is not observed.

\paragraph{Why tip-only fails on DS1.}
Tip velocity across a window cannot identify the per-segment bending split (multi-valued inverse). The recurrent network receives a richer input with no reduction in under-identification and collapses to the same posterior mean; best-epoch index falls from $15$ ($K=5$) to $1$ ($K=20$), consistent with accelerated over-fitting rather than signal recovery.

\paragraph{Why tip-plus-mid benefits.}
Once the mid marker pins internal state at every frame, the $K$-frame window carries internal-state velocity and acceleration. The $R^{2}$ $0.44 \to 0.51$ magnitude suggests a hysteretic signal is present but not dominant; a structured temporal protocol or a Preisach/Bouc-Wen parametrisation would be needed to isolate it.

\paragraph{Pneumatic transients set the window length.}
Alessi et al.\ \cite{alessi2024pushing} Table~1 report asymmetric AM-I-Support transients ($0.2$\,s pressurisation / $0.56$\,s depressurisation), which at DS1's $20$\,Hz correspond to $\sim$$4$ / $\sim$$11$ frames---above the single-frame MLP's horizon but within $K \geq 10$ of the LSTM. Empirical cross-correlation on DS1 (Fig~\ref{fig:app_ds1_lag}) gives a median lag of $13$ frames with chamber~$5$ peaking at $0.25$\,s ($R = 0.87$), consistent with Alessi's pressurisation value to within one frame.

\paragraph{Why DS2 benefits only marginally.}
DS2 is IID (step-size ratio $1.00$/$0.96$); the $4.8\%$ MAE reduction cannot reflect captured hysteresis. A regularisation story fits better: neighbouring rows with similar tips supply local averaging, giving the monotone MAE improvement with longer $K$ ($148.05 \to 138.68$) without any genuine temporal signal.

On the reference trajectories the recurrent and feed-forward networks were within one millimetre of each other in tracking error, but the LSTM's pressure-command jerk was one to two orders of magnitude lower on Dataset~1---a property of the hidden-state low-pass, useful where actuator wear or valve chatter matters.

\section{Jacobian-learned iteration versus direct regression}
\label{sec:disc_jacobian_vs_mlp}

Direct regression is the posterior-mean estimator (minimises pressure error); Jacobian iteration commits to a single posterior-mode branch (minimises position error). Below are four operational axes.

\paragraph{Generalisation.}
Direct regression is bounded by tip-space training coverage; Jacobian iteration by FK-network accuracy on solver iterates. Consequences: Jacobian inherits FK failure modes (Dataset~2 FK MAE $2.55$\,mm caps achievable closed-loop error at that scale) but can in principle reach targets outside the IK training distribution, as demonstrated by sub-mm results on Dataset~1's unseen seed-0 test sub-sample.

\paragraph{Sample efficiency.}
The forward map is single-valued regardless of actuation redundancy and therefore cheaper to learn than the inverse (Dataset~1 FK reached best epoch at $2$, against $4$ for the IK network). Small-data regimes should favour Jacobian iteration; a systematic downsampled sweep is a natural follow-up.

\paragraph{OOD robustness is the honest cost.}
Dataset~2 spiral: warm-start Jacobian $31.94$\,mm (worst case $258$\,mm), against $7.42$\,mm Sim2Real and $8.17$\,mm tip-only. A single diverging DLS run on a three-valve arm invalidates the mean because there is no redundancy to recover; feed-forward passes cannot diverge. The sub-mm advantage is therefore conditional on staying within well-conditioned FK regions, and requires explicit workspace bounds or safety checks in production.

\paragraph{Runtime.}
$46$\,ms per target against $<1$\,ms feed-forward: $20$\,Hz OK, $1$\,kHz not. Quasi-static pick-and-place favours Jacobian; kHz dynamics requires feed-forward unless the FK evaluation can be specialised (e.g.\ a Taylor expansion replacing the autograd Jacobian in a fixed neighbourhood).

\paragraph{The hybrid is already default.}
Every headline Jacobian number uses warm-start from the direct regressor: on Dataset~2 this cut mean iterations $16.8 \to 2.3$ and lifted convergence $78 \to 99\%$. Natural extensions: nearest-neighbour warm-start from a cached training distribution, a learned divergence-early-exit, and a two-stage controller that triggers Jacobian only on threshold exceedance.

\section{Sim-to-real observations}
\label{sec:disc_sim}

Strategy~B matched Strategy~A within $\pm 1$ command unit at every budget; Strategy~C reached $R^{2} = -1.71$. Two findings, one diagnosis: the reality gap here is dominated by geometric workspace mismatch, not by sample count or material fidelity.

\paragraph{Workspace geometry dominates the reality gap.}
The synthetic tip cloud spans $\approx 500 \times 480 \times 330$\,mm against Dataset~2's $102 \times 104 \times 64$\,mm; the two distributions essentially do not overlap. Other axes---hyperelasticity, viscoelastic hysteresis, pneumatic lag (absent via Eq.~\eqref{eq:sim_moment}), manufacturing asymmetry---contribute in principle, but Strategy~C's $R^{2} = -1.71$ is set by the coordinate mismatch: a network trained in the wrong region cannot map real inputs to the right output regardless of material fidelity. Strategy~B's fine-tuning then discards the pre-training because the synthetic input range has no counterpart in the real distribution (a $(150,-200,-300)$\,mm synthetic tip has no Dataset~2 analogue).

\paragraph{Why Fang et al.\ succeeded.}
Fang et al.\ \cite{fang2022jacobian} used a digital twin of the target platform. The simulator here was a Manti-inspired reference rod because Dataset~2's parameters are undocumented. The negative result is therefore an argument for Fang's method \emph{conditional on a digital twin}, not a refutation.

\paragraph{Minimum changes for a positive transfer.}
(1)~Rescale and re-centre the simulator workspace to the target; this removes the dominant axis. (2)~Calibrate pressure-to-command scale so synthetic commands cover the recorded range. (3)~Add a hysteresis term (e.g.\ a Preisach layer). The first is most tractable; the other two address axes the present design could not isolate.

\section{Limitations}
\label{sec:disc_limitations}

\paragraph{No physical robot.}
\label{sec:disc_lim_hw}
Every closed-loop number was measured through the shared learned FK arbiter (test MAE $2.55$\,mm DS2, $12.97$\,mm DS1), which itself caps the resolvable between-method accuracy. Controller latency, sensor noise, and actuator saturation are untested; the Dataset~2 spiral divergence may or may not stabilise under real closed-loop feedback. Closing this limitation requires physical access to Dataset~1 or Dataset~2's arm.

\paragraph{Dataset coverage.}
\label{sec:disc_lim_data}
Two pneumatic platforms with optical markers, workspaces at most a few hundred millimetres. Untested: cable- and tendon-driven arms with different hysteresis; metre-scale arms with different noise-to-workspace ratios; full-shape sensors (fibre-Bragg, proprioceptive cells) for which the discrete mid-marker ablation may not directly translate. The three-property decision framework rests on $n = 2$ datasets.

\paragraph{Simulator fidelity.}
\label{sec:disc_lim_sim}
The PyElastica rod is single-module, linear-elastic, instantaneous pressure-as-moment, without viscoelasticity or manufacturing imperfections. Of the five reality-gap axes only workspace geometry was measured quantitatively; the negative Dataset~2 sim-to-real result is therefore consistent with any combination of gaps contributing. The Young's-modulus calibration used Manti anchors, not Dataset~2 data; the rod is an I-Support reference, not a Dataset~2 model. A follow-up with hyperelasticity, viscoelastic damping, and a pneumatic-line delay would be the minimal platform for per-axis attribution.

\paragraph{Metric choice and statistical evidence.}
\label{sec:disc_lim_metric}
All position errors use the tip only; differently-shaped arms with the same tip are scored identically, and a Hausdorff-distance full-shape metric would be more appropriate for obstacle-avoidance or contact tasks. Pressure metrics on multi-valued inverses favour posterior-mean estimators and do not rank by a single notion of ``quality''. Networks were trained once per configuration (single seed); the paired Wilcoxon + bootstrap CI on the mid-marker ablation is the only reported uncertainty quantification. Retraining every configuration from $3$--$5$ independent seeds would tighten the Jacobian-vs-MLP and overall comparisons at roughly $3\times$ the compute cost.

\section{Implications}
\label{sec:disc_implications}

\paragraph{Method selection checklist.}
(1)~Count actuation vs output DOF: if actuators outnumber outputs, the inverse is multi-valued and warm-started Jacobian iteration reaches the tolerance floor at $\sim$$2\times 10^{2}$ slower per target; if they match, direct regression is simpler with comparable accuracy. (2)~Assess deployment-time partial observations: on multi-segment arms a single inter-module marker triad beats any tested network architecture change. (3)~If a simulator is available, measure synthetic--real workspace overlap before any pre-training; with small overlap (as on Dataset~2), the sim prior will not help at any fine-tuning budget.

\paragraph{Hardware / sensor design.}
Invest in partial-shape sensing rather than deeper networks or larger datasets: one inter-module marker triad lifts Dataset~1 $R^{2}$ from $0.32$ to $0.51$. Document actuation units, straight-pose origin, and a calibration sequence with every new dataset---the $6.9\times$ Ma-benchmark gap here was a metadata gap, not a method-quality gap. For closed-loop deployment, continuous shape sensors (fibre-Bragg, proprioceptive cells) remove the deployment-time OOD risk that tripped the tip-plus-mid network when the mid signal was unavailable ($19.08$\,mm tracking error).

\paragraph{Simulation / sim-to-real.}
Workspace rescaling to match the target distribution is the cheapest intervention and the only one likely to convert a DS2-style negative transfer. Hyperelastic material laws and viscoelastic damping are expensive to calibrate; add them only once a workspace-aligned simulator has been shown to help. When a digital twin is not achievable, compute is better spent on real-data recording than on richer simulation.

\paragraph{Control engineering.}
Pick the method by the downstream metric: tip-position feedback $\to$ warm-start Jacobian-IK; pressure-MAPE reporting $\to$ direct regression (also the only kHz option). The default hybrid (FF warm-start + DLS refinement) is faster and more accurate than either extreme; a two-stage controller that triggers Jacobian only on error-threshold exceedance would get both. Keep the LSTM where actuator wear or valve chatter matters: its jerk is $1$--$2$ orders of magnitude below the feed-forward alternative on Dataset~1.

\paragraph{Broader soft-robotics research.}
Three compact claims. (1)~Classical PCC is not competitive with a small feed-forward network on either pneumatic dataset---constant-$L$ ceiling on Dataset~2, multi-valued-inverse calibration collapse on Dataset~1; PCC is a useful reference, not a deployable method. (2)~The single biggest IK-pipeline improvement in this thesis came from three marker coordinates, not from any architecture change; partial-shape sensing is higher-leverage than deeper networks. (3)~Sim-to-real needs a workspace-aligned simulator; architecturally similar rods are not drop-in priors on undocumented platforms.
